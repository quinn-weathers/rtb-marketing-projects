Top of mind · the week07 · 10

The week Virginia went from “muddled” to blocked statewide — on a schedule. A Washington County judge expanded the Santolla v. Katz injunction into a universal injunction barring every Virginia law-enforcement agency and Commonwealth’s Attorney from enforcing the “assault firearms” and magazine bans, effective July 21 and reported to run through July 2027 — past the expected Supreme Court AR-15 decision.

The arc of the week was Virginia’s: Monday a three-judge panel denied the Commonwealth’s bid to consolidate the four ban lawsuits (“too late to prevent inconsistent rulings”), unblocking the NSSF suit; Thursday the statewide injunction landed, scheduling an end to the named-defendant-only patchwork era. Around it, the judicial track kept accelerating: the confirmed DOJ suit against California over the AB 1127 “Glock ban” and handgun roster sits fully briefed awaiting a TRO ruling; the Seventh Circuit’s U.S. v. Rose extended the Rahimi/Hemani “present danger” logic to the lifetime mental-health ban; the Third Circuit ordered fast supplemental briefing on Wolford and Hemani in New Jersey’s AR-15 case; and Viramontes settled into its analysis phase, with a cross-ideological scholar consensus expecting the AR-15 bans to fall and the real debate shifting to breadth. Demand followed the law: June NICS came in +11.7% with NFA checks up 177% on the $0 tax stamp. The empowerment read: members plan around dates now on the calendar — July 21 in Virginia, Aug. 1 in Colorado, a fall argument at the Court — not around fear of what might happen.

Moved State · Virginia Heat87▲ Jul 6 – Jul 9 · AmmoLand, VA OAG, NRA-ILA, GOF

Virginia, in one week: consolidation DENIED Monday, statewide injunction Thursday — the “muddled” era is scheduled to end July 21

The week opened with a three-judge panel denying the Commonwealth’s motion to consolidate and transfer the four “assault firearms” ban suits (Santolla, Crump, Curtis, Black v. Hook) — common questions don’t predominate, the cases run on different legal theories, and it was “too late to prevent inconsistent rulings.” Each case proceeds in its own court, and the NSSF-backed suit was unblocked. Thursday brought the bigger move: Washington County Circuit Judge Jeffrey L. Campbell expanded the Santolla v. Katz injunction into a universal injunction covering every law-enforcement agency and Commonwealth’s Attorney, effective July 21, reported to run through July 2027; Attorney General Jones’s bid to narrow the scope was rejected as inviting a “treacherous patchwork.” The statewide order is reported by AmmoLand and NRA-ILA; cross-confirmation from The Reload was still pending at press time. Heat rose across the week (86→89→83→91) — the only lead story that finished hotter than it started.

Ongoing SCOTUS · Merits case Heat91▼ Jul 6 – Jul 9 · The Reload, AmmoLand, SCOTUS docket

Viramontes enters its analysis phase — seven scholars expect the AR-15 bans to fall; the live debate is breadth, and the wait is “strategy, not a snub”

With cert granted (No. 25-238, consolidated with Grant v. Higgins, No. 25-566), the week belonged to the analysts. The Reload’s survey of seven cross-ideological Second Amendment scholars (Kopel, Willinger, Blackman, Lawrence, Francis, Mocsary, Ruben) found a consensus the Court is likely to strike the AR-15 bans — Mocsary and Ruben declined to predict — with Chief Justice Roberts and Justice Barrett the votes to watch and breadth, not outcome, the open question. Companion analysis laid out the oral-argument tells: the common-use test, the level of generality, Solicitor General participation, and whether the ruling stays firearms-only. Late-week commentary hardened the “strategy, not a snub” read of the fall schedule: argument expected in the October 2026 term, decision by June 2027. Heat stayed volcanic all four days (95→92→90→88), cooling only as the story matured from event to backdrop.

Ongoing Courts · Doctrine Heat72▼ Jul 6 – Jul 9 · The Reload, SCOTUS dockets, CA filings

The “common use” test ran through every case on this page — from the Virginia injunction to California’s TRO brief to the Viramontes question presented

The doctrinal thread of the week. The same Heller “common use” question anchors the Viramontes merits, DOJ’s Glock-ban complaint (which pleads Heller common-use directly), and California’s response — the state argues Glocks with legal factory configurations fail Bruen step one, that there is “no right to factory settings,” and that the banned configurations are “dangerous and unusual,” citing the Ninth Circuit’s Duncan. How the Court frames common use in Viramontes will cascade into the magazine cases, the Glock litigation, and every AWB challenge now pending. Sustained hot-to-warm heat (78→74→70→64) as the framing settled in.

CONFIRMED: DOJ sued California over the AB 1127 “Glock ban” and the handgun roster — fully briefed, TRO ruling awaited

Last week’s Unverified resolved: DOJ filed United States v. California & Bonta on July 1 over AB 1127 (the “Glock ban”) and the handgun roster, seeking an ex parte TRO on Heller common-use grounds. California opposed on July 3 — Glocks in the banned configuration fail Bruen step one, “no right to factory settings,” “dangerous and unusual” — and the motion sat fully briefed through the week, at day six of ruling silence by Thursday. A second Glock-ban case, NRA v. Moore (Maryland), is running in parallel. This is DOJ’s third state-level 2A enforcement front (Colorado, Virginia, California). New Courts · 7th Circuit Heat69▼ Jul 7 – Jul 9 · The Reload, AmmoLand, CourtListener

U.S. v. Rose (7th Cir.) — the lifetime mental-health gun ban survives facially but opens to as-applied challenges absent “present danger”

Cross-confirmed midweek (Reload + AmmoLand): in U.S. v. Rose (No. 24-1086, decided July 2), a Seventh Circuit panel opinion by Judge Frank Easterbrook (appointed by President Reagan) held §922(g)(4)’s lifetime ban after a mental-health commitment facially constitutional but open to as-applied challenges — the government must be able to show something like a present danger, not rely on a decades-old commitment alone; the panel vacated and remanded. It extends the Rahimi/Hemani/Wolford “present danger” line into the mental-health prohibitor and connects to the §925(c) rights-restoration rebuild. Heat: debut 68, peak 75, settling to 65 as it entered the doctrine conversation.
