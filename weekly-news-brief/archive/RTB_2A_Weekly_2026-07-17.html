Top of mind · the week07 · 17

Countdown week: Virginia holds its July 21 date through three quiet cycles, Congress finally scores — the House passes H.R. 1181, the merchant-code ban, 221–201 — and the NFA registry fight opens a civil flank in four courts at once.

The week’s defining feature was a calendar, not a crisis. Virginia’s cross-confirmed statewide injunction held steady toward its July 21 effective date — dealers queued held orders, AG Jones’s Crump appeal sat at the Virginia Supreme Court with the leading analysis expecting a punt until the Supreme Court speaks, and the only missing operational piece was NSSF’s dealer guidance. Around that countdown, the judicial track kept producing: The Reload’s analysis reframed Barnett v. Raoul (the Seventh Circuit upheld Illinois’ AWB but abandoned the old “not arms” rationale); DOJ’s California TRO was denied without any Second Amendment analysis — on §12601 standing and pre-ban inventory; and the NRA/FPC/SAF/ASA Machamer amicus plus SAF’s supplemental filings in three district courts turned the $0 tax stamp into a registry-wide constitutional attack. Thursday, Congress moved for the first time in 45 days: H.R. 1181 would end gun-retailer merchant category codes nationwide, and Sen. Graham’s death — now officially confirmed — left the FFL Protection Act looking for a sponsor. The empowerment read: members plan around dates on a calendar — July 21 in Virginia, Aug. 1 in Colorado, Aug. 4 at ATF, October at the Court — not around fear of what might happen.

Moved Courts · 7th Circuit Heat78▼▼ Jul 13 – Jul 16 · The Reload, AmmoLand, C.A.7

Barnett v. Raoul, reframed — the Seventh Circuit upheld Illinois’ ban but abandoned Bevis’s “not arms” rationale; by Thursday the opinion had settled into pre-Viramontes reference material

Monday’s Reload analysis materially revised the picture of Judge Amy St. Eve’s majority (St. Eve, a Trump appointee): rather than doubling down on Bevis’s M16-equivalence holding, the court assumed without deciding that AR-15s and 30-round magazines are protected arms — then upheld the Protect Illinois Communities Act on the historical tradition other circuits have “coalesced around”: targeted restrictions on weapons whose “danger and lethality” stand out. That step-one concession is exactly the ground Viramontes will test, and the St. Eve-vs.-Brennan split is now the cleanest single frame for explaining what the Supreme Court took the case to resolve. Heat told the lifecycle story: Monday’s 89 (the week’s co-peak) cooled to 76 and then 70 as the analysis cycle completed — a story maturing, not dying.

New Courts · NFA registry Heat75▲ Jul 15 – Jul 16 · AmmoLand, 6th Cir., district filings

The NFA registry offensive — the week’s only riser: the NRA/FPC/SAF/ASA Machamer amicus plus SAF supplementals in three district courts argue the $0-tax registry lost its constitutional footing

Two coordinated moves turned the post-tax-repeal question into a four-court offensive. Wednesday, the NRA, FPC, SAF and the American Suppressor Association filed a Sixth Circuit amicus in U.S. v. Machamer (NRA’s Joseph G.S. Greenlee authoring) attacking the NFA’s approval/registration provisions as applied to SBRs — 1.18 million registered SBRs, the brief argues, forecloses “unusual,” and Heller/Bruen/Wolford put the plain-text step on bearable arms. Thursday, SAF’s supplemental filings in Brown, Jensen (Silencer Shop Foundation consolidation) and Roberts v. ATF added the taxing-power flank: with the One Big Beautiful Bill zeroing the making-and-transfer tax, maintaining registration on untaxed citizens would let Congress “regulate in minute detail the activities of untaxed Americans” — and Landor, SAF argues, forecloses the Necessary-and-Proper rescue. Rising heat (72→78), and the demand data (+177% YoY NFA checks) now doubles as courtroom evidence.

Moved Executive · DOJ v. California Heat66▼▼ Jul 13 – Jul 16 · AmmoLand, DOJ, CA AG

DOJ’s “Glock ban” TRO is DENIED — on §12601 standing and pre-ban inventory, with no Second Amendment analysis at all; the case now turns on whether DOJ can sue in the first place

Last week’s “likeliest catalyst” landed Monday, and it was a procedural ruling, not a merits one. Judge Mónica Ramírez Almadani (C.D. Cal., a Biden appointee) declined to reach the Second Amendment: she ordered additional briefing on whether DOJ has standing to challenge a state gun law under 34 U.S.C. §12601 — the 1994 police-misconduct statute the Civil Rights Division invoked — and found no irreparable harm because dealers may still sell cruciform-trigger pistols acquired before January 1, 2026. AB 1127 stays in force; the “Glock ban” question is now a §12601 standing puzzle that will decide whether DOJ’s three-state 2A enforcement strategy (Colorado, Virginia, California) has a viable vehicle at all. Heat cooled through the week as the briefing wait set in (74→66→58).
