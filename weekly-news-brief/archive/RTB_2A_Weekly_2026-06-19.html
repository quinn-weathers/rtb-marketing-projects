The courts ran the week. With Congress consumed by reconciliation and FISA, every story that stayed hot Mon–Fri lived at the Supreme Court, in the circuits, or in a state AG’s office — and the term’s marquee carry case, Wolford v. Lopez, is still pending as the Court races to clear its docket. For RTB members the throughline is calm and practical: know the law that governs you today, and know which decisions could change it in the next two weeks.

What this week was. Four of the five hottest items on the board were judicial. Wolford v. Lopez (Hawaii’s private-property carry default) held the #1 slot all week as a decision-watch, averaging 88 and still rising into Friday — no opinion had posted as of this brief, so it remains awaiting opinion, not decided (treat as imminent). Monday’s orders list cleared two New York challenges (Corbett v. Hochul and NSSF v. James) on cert denials while granting four new 2A cases and relisting 25. And two state-level stories — Virginia’s July 1 hardware ban and Florida’s collapsing waiting-period law — gave members concrete, date-certain things to plan around.

The empowerment frame. This was a “what the law actually says” week, not a scary-headlines week. Florida is easing restrictions through litigation; the Washington DUI ruling is narrow and split 5–4; the Harden arrest is a clean teachable moment about transport rules, not a crisis. RTB’s job into next week is to be the calm, precise voice while the Court finishes its term.

Florida’s gun-purchase waiting periods are ending — AG Uthmeier declines to defend, agrees to a judgment blocking them

The most-anticipated item on the board, #1 every reading-day and still rising into Friday. SCOTUS Monday orders: cert denied in Corbett v. Hochul & NSSF v. James; four 2A cases granted, 25 relisted

Monday’s orders cleared the decks. The Court declined Corbett v. Hochul (whether Bruen’s footnote 9 lets a shall-issue regime impose a training mandate) and NSSF v. James (New York’s PLCAA-weakening liability statute). Both were at the preliminary-injunction stage, where the Court has been reluctant to intervene, so the laws stay in force and the fights return to the lower courts on the merits — with no reasons given. The Court also granted four new 2A cases and relisted 25, leaving 29 challenges on its books. Heat spiked Thursday on the report, then eased Friday.

Watch ▼ Courts · Washington Heat60▼ All week · The Reload (Fogleman), WA Supreme Court

Washington Supreme Court (5–4): repeat DUI convictions can trigger disarmament without an individualized dangerousness finding

In McLellan & Holloway v. Brown, the Washington Supreme Court held the state may temporarily disarm people convicted of two-plus DUIs within seven years under a 2023 law (restoration petition available after five law-abiding years). Justice Steven González (for the majority) concluded the legislature may disarm a category by conviction type without proving each person dangerous, citing traditions of disarming “dangerous” groups and distinguishing Rahimi. The four-justice dissent (Justice Helen Whitener) countered there is “no historical analog for driving under the influence.” Narrow on its facts, but the categorical-dangerousness reasoning could carry — and the federal counterpart awaits en banc review at the Third Circuit (Williams). Steady fade across the week.

Gun groups unite against NBA star James Harden’s Texas arrest over an unholstered gun in his car

A late-week breakout and the cleanest teachable moment of the week. Houston police arrested Cleveland Cavaliers guard James Harden on a misdemeanor unlawful-carry count — not for lacking a permit (Texas has permitless carry since 2021) but for a little-known exception barring an unholstered handgun in a vehicle. The NRA, CCRKBA, GOA and FPC condemned the arrest; the NRA urged Texas lawmakers to repeal the provision. Harden, released on a $100 bond, is due back in court June 22. Entered the board Thursday at 80, eased Friday.
