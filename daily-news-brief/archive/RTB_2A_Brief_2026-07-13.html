The weekend rewrote what Barnett actually held. The Reload’s full analysis of the opinion shows the Seventh Circuit abandoned its old “AR-15s aren’t arms” rationale — the panel assumed the rifles and magazines are protected, then upheld Illinois’ ban anyway on a “particularly dangerous weapons” historical tradition. That reframing, not the outcome, is what the justices will be reading this fall.

What changed over the weekend. Friday’s single-source picture is now cross-confirmed and sharper. Per Jake Fogleman’s July 12 analysis of the opinion text, Judge St. Eve’s majority jettisoned Bevis’s step-one holding (AR-15s as M16-equivalents outside the Second Amendment’s text) — the reasoning Justice Thomas once called “nonsensical” — and instead assumed without deciding that AR-15s and 30-round magazines are protected arms. The ban survived at Bruen step two: a claimed tradition of restricting “particularly dangerous weapons,” with 19th-century Bowie-knife laws as the “leading example” (ten carry restrictions, two taxes — and just two outright sales bans), quoting Rahimi that a modern law “does not need to be” identical to its historical analogues. Chief Judge Brennan’s 50-page dissent answers that the case “should begin and end” with common use: “because the people have overwhelmingly chosen the AR-15 rifle and its magazine… they are protected by the Second Amendment.”

Why it matters. Barnett is likely the last merits ruling on hardware bans before SCOTUS decides Viramontes/Grant — the freshest lower-court analysis the justices will have in front of them, built (per Brennan) on “perhaps the most comprehensive trial record in any Second Amendment case to date.” The fall argument now has its two roadmaps in one opinion: St. Eve’s flexible historical approach vs. Brennan’s strict common-use reading. Illinois’ ban stays enforceable in the interim; within a year, Viramontes controls either way.

The second story: California’s “Glock ban” survives its first test — without a single word of Second Amendment analysis. Judge Mónica Ramírez Almadani (C.D. Cal.) denied the DOJ’s TRO against AB 1127 on procedural grounds: she ordered further briefing on whether DOJ even has standing to sue under 34 U.S.C. §12601 (a 1994 police-misconduct statute), and found no irreparable harm because dealers can still sell pre-2026 cruciform-trigger inventory. No Bruen analysis, no merits ruling — the ban stays in force, the constitutional question is punted, and a TRO/PI can be revisited after briefing. Meanwhile GOA opened a new front Sunday: a challenge to Pennsylvania’s lifetime carry-license ban for Air Force veteran Craig Philips over a 1994 marijuana conviction, leaning on Bruen and this term’s Hemani drug-user ruling (single-source: AmmoLand).

Barnett v. Raoul, cross-confirmed and reframed — the majority abandoned Bevis’s “not arms” rationale and upheld Illinois’ ban at Bruen step two on a “particularly dangerous weapons” tradition

The Reload’s July 12 opinion analysis materially revises Friday’s picture. Judge St. Eve’s majority did not double down on Bevis’s M16-equivalence holding — it assumed without deciding that AR-15s and 30-round magazines are protected arms, then upheld the Protect Illinois Communities Act on the historical tradition other circuits have “coalesced around”: targeted restrictions on weapons “whose danger and lethality stand out,” with 19th-century Bowie-knife laws the “leading example” and Rahimi’s “does not need to be identical” standard doing the analytical work.
