The Court ruled. SCOTUS handed down its first major Second Amendment decision of the term on June 18 — a unanimous, deliberately narrow win in US v. Hemani striking down the federal drug-user gun ban as applied to a regular marijuana user. With Hemani decided, Wolford v. Lopez is now the lone outstanding 2A merits case as the term closes this week. Meanwhile a Fifth Circuit panel declared silencers protected “Arms,” and Virginia’s hardware ban is now T-8 to July 1.

Hemani, decided 9–0. Justice Gorsuch, writing for a unanimous Court, held that the government failed to justify prosecuting Ali Hemani under 18 U.S.C. §922(g)(3) (the ban on gun possession by “unlawful users” of controlled substances) merely for admitted, roughly every-other-day marijuana use. The Court stressed the ruling is narrow — tied to this defendant — and explicitly did not reach bans on addicts or the presently intoxicated, §922(g)(1) felony disarmament, or prosecutions backed by individualized proof of dangerousness. But with roughly three-quarters of Americans living in states with legal marijuana, the practical reach is large. Concurrences: Thomas (doubting §922(g)(3) under the Commerce Clause), Jackson joined by Sotomayor (calling the Bruen method “unworkable”), and Alito joined by Kagan (preferring an even narrower decision).

Wolford is now the watch. With Hemani down, Wolford v. Lopez — testing Hawaii’s default rule barring carry on private property open to the public without express permission — stands as the sole pending Second Amendment merits case. The term traditionally wraps by the end of June, so a decision could land any opinion morning. Keep the Wolford decoder pre-positioned; treat as imminent, not decided.

Fifth Circuit: silencers are “Arms.” In US v. Comeaux, a three-judge panel unanimously held that suppressors are protected Second Amendment “Arms” — but upheld the NFA registration requirement anyway under the circuit’s Peterson precedent (the Bruen footnote-9 shall-issue presumption). The “arms” holding creates a circuit split that could draw Supreme Court interest, even as the conviction stood.

Virginia, T-8. The ban takes effect July 1. New wrinkle this week: gun-rights advocates are now turning the “collective/militia right” framing — long a gun-control argument — back against Virginia’s AG in the Crump v. Katz litigation. Watch for any preliminary-injunction ruling before the deadline.

New ▲ Courts · 5th Circuit Heat77▲ June 23 · The Reload (Gutowski), CourtListener

Fifth Circuit: silencers ARE Second Amendment “Arms” — but NFA registration stands (US v. Comeaux)

A unanimous Fifth Circuit panel (Judge Jerry E. Smith) upheld Brennan Comeaux’s conviction for possessing unregistered silencers — while squarely deciding, for the first time in that circuit, that suppressors are protected “Arms” because they “facilitate armed self-defense” (reduced recoil, hearing protection, accuracy). That rejects DOJ’s position that silencers aren’t “arms” and creates a circuit split that could draw Supreme Court interest. The conviction nonetheless stood: under the circuit’s binding Peterson precedent, Comeaux had to show the NFA’s shall-issue registration regime was “put toward abusive ends” (exorbitant fees, lengthy waits) and did not. A concurrence by Judge Edith Brown Clement (joined by Judge Kyle Duncan) sharply criticized Peterson’s reliance on the Bruen footnote-9 presumption and urged the full court to revisit it en banc.

RTB angle: Pair with the S. 218 explainer. The teachable point: a court calling silencers “Arms” is legally significant, but it did not strike the NFA — registration still applies. Track for a possible en banc petition or SCOTUS cert; describe the split plainly, no overpromising.
