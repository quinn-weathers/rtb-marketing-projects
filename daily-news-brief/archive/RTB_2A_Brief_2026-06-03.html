Wednesday open: Virginia’s background-check standoff goes before the judge today. Last evening, VCDL and GOA published a joint letter warning Virginia law-enforcement officers and prosecutors that they could face personal liability — with qualified immunity arguably unavailable — if they enforce the state’s universal background-check law while Judge F. Patrick Yeatts’s permanent injunction stands. Oral arguments on the competing motions (the groups’ contempt motion vs. the Attorney General’s motion to dissolve the injunction as moot) are set for Wednesday in Lynchburg Circuit Court.

How the showdown got here. Judge Yeatts struck down Virginia’s 2020 universal background-check law (Va. Code § 18.2-308.2:5) last October, ruling it effectively barred 18-to-20-year-olds from legally buying handguns because the FBI won’t run checks on pistol sales to under-21s. The 2026 General Assembly responded with HB 1525 — prohibiting 18-to-20-year-olds from buying handguns outright — signed by Gov. Spanberger with a procedurally contested emergency clause. AG Jay Jones then directed Virginia State Police to resume private-sale checks; VCDL/GOA filed for contempt, arguing only the Lynchburg court (or a higher court) can dissolve its own injunction (“not the General Assembly by statute, and certainly not the Attorney General by fiat”). Today’s hearing should bring the first judicial read on which side is right.

Why it matters beyond Virginia. This is now the cleanest live test of a question echoing across several states: can a legislature legislate around a standing court injunction? It also collides with the existing VA storylines — the four-suit hardware-ban litigation (effective July 1, T-28) and the four-county prosecutor-refusal substory — making Virginia the highest-attention state in the space this morning.

Continuing threads. Congress is in Day 3 back in session; still no leadership floor signal on H.R. 38 / H.R. 4112 reciprocity or the Hearing Protection Act (S. 218). SCOTUS holds a conference Thursday June 4 and has an opinion day set for Monday June 8 — with 26 decisions pending, Wolford v. Lopez (Hawaii “Vampire Rule”) could land any opinion day from here to the end of June. The DOJ Sec. 925(c) rights-restoration rule remains unpublished. Colorado’s SB25-003 draft-list comment window is closed (final guidance July 1); Initiative 89’s signature outcome remains unverified. NSSF’s May NICS-adjusted figures are still expected this week, and NSSF’s Gun Storage Check Week (June 1–7) is running now.

What this means for RTB content posture today. The Virginia story is a high-care, high-value explainer: VA members face genuine legal ambiguity about private-sale transfers right now, and the responsible move is a calm “what we know, what’s disputed, what to do until the court rules” piece — recommend members verify current requirements before any private transfer rather than acting on either side’s characterization. Hold publication of anything definitive until after today’s hearing if possible. The reciprocity/travel cluster and the Wolford pre-positioned explainer remain the evergreen plays.
