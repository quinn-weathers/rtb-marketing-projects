Top of mind07 · 02

The three biggest items we carried as Unverified for days all broke — and they broke big. The Supreme Court granted cert in the AR-15 “assault weapon” ban cases (Viramontes v. Cook County and Grant v. Higgins, consolidated), agreeing to decide for the first time whether the Second Amendment protects AR-15-platform rifles. The same orders held the magazine cases and denied the young-adult cases. Meanwhile the DOJ sued Virginia over its ban, and Virginia’s “assault firearms” ban took effect July 1 into a “muddled mess” of partial injunctions — as June rifle sales hit an all-time record.

The headline: SCOTUS will decide the AR-15 question. On June 30 the Court granted certiorari and consolidated Viramontes v. Cook County (Illinois) and Grant v. Higgins (Connecticut), taking up “whether the Second and Fourteenth Amendments guarantee the right to possess AR-15 platform and similar semi-automatic rifles.” The Seventh and Second Circuits had upheld both bans last summer; the Second Amendment Foundation is a plaintiff in both. This is the first time the Court has agreed to hear the merits of an “assault weapon” ban — making good on Justice Kavanaugh’s 2025 promise that it would reach the AR-15 issue “in the next Term or two.” Oral argument is expected this fall; a decision could come any time, though the term likely runs to June 2027. Cross-confirmed: The Reload and the Court’s June 30 orders list.

What the Court did not do matters too. The justices did not act on the magazine-capacity cases — including the decade-long Duncan v. Bonta (California) and Gator’s Custom Guns (Washington) — and will presumably hold them until the AR-15 case is decided. And the Court denied review in roughly a half-dozen cases on 18-to-20-year-old purchase, possession and carry restrictions, leaving a 3–3 circuit split intact. So the cert cluster we carried as Unverified for three runs resolves as: AR-15 granted, magazines held, young-adult denied.

Virginia: the ban is “in effect,” but the picture is murky. The “assault firearms” ban took effect July 1. Gun-rights groups have won two preliminary injunctions — the NRA secured a statewide injunction (June 29) against the Virginia State Police and six Commonwealth’s Attorneys — but the Attorney General’s office says those injunctions bind only the named defendants, so the law “remains in force” against everyone else, including non-defendant local prosecutors. Ken Cuccinelli says the ban is still live in “125+ localities.” The result: retailers statewide have pulled AR-15s rather than risk liability, and NSSF’s Larry Keane called it “a bit of a muddled mess.” AG Jay Jones (D.) will seek an emergency stay and appeal. A hearing on consolidating the challenges is set for Monday, July 6.

DOJ opens a third front — Virginia. The Justice Department’s Civil Rights Division filed a new case against Virginia’s ban, making Virginia the third AR-15-ban jurisdiction DOJ has sued (after DC and Colorado). AAG Harmeet Dhillon framed it as protecting the right “to possess arms owned by millions.” Separately, the DOJ–California “Glock ban” (AB 1127) / handgun-roster pre-suit deadline (5 p.m. ET June 30) — whether a complaint was filed or talks opened — could not be confirmed from reachable sources this run and stays Unverified.

Record demand. The FBI’s June NICS report (released July 1) shows Virginia processed 43,553 long-gun checks in June — a new state record, up more than 3.5× year-over-year, and the most long-gun checks of any state, ahead of Texas, California and Florida despite their far larger populations. Demand for the soon-to-be-restricted rifles more than tripled to an all-time high ahead of the effective date.

Verification note. The Reload was current through the evening of July 1 and is cross-confirmed by the Court’s orders list, the DOJ and NRA-ILA statements, the Virginia AG’s July 1 litigation update, and the FBI NICS release. Still Unverified this run: the DOJ–California filing posture after the June 30 deadline, and Colorado’s SB25-003 final “Specified Semiautomatic Firearms” list contents and the Initiative 89 signature-qualification outcome.
