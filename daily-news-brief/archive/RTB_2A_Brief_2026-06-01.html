Top of mind06 · 01

Monday open: the weekend’s big strategic takeaway from The Reload is that national carry reciprocity is not going to come from the courts — it will have to come from Congress, which returns from its Memorial Day recess today. The Eighth Circuit’s unanimous ruling in Johnson v. Jacobson held that a state’s reciprocity statute “standing alone, does not regulate arms-bearing conduct,” failing at Bruen step one. Jake Fogleman’s Sunday analysis frames the lesson plainly: the post-Bruen fight to end non-resident carry bans was an unbroken winning streak, but forcing states to honor each other’s permits by lawsuit is a far harder lift — even before a sympathetic court.

Why this matters for the legislative calendar. With the judicial path to reciprocity now looking foreclosed, the only realistic route to national portability runs through bills like H.R. 38 (Concealed Carry Reciprocity Act) and the Hudson companion H.R. 4112 — both of which re-enter the active calendar as the House and Senate return today. Judge L. Steven Grasz’s opinion reasoned that because Minnesota issues shall-issue permits to residents and non-residents alike (a system Bruen blessed), its choice to recognize only 33 states’ permits is “interstate comity, not the Second Amendment.” SAF’s Kostas Moros called the loss unsurprising given thin briefing on historical “traveler’s exceptions” — but Fogleman notes that even well-briefed reciprocity claims (e.g., CRPA v. LASD, where Judge Garnett rejected an Obergefell marriage-license analogy) have lost on the same logic.

The DOJ rights-restoration rule is still missing — seven months after comments closed. Gutowski’s analysis (carried into the weekend members’ newsletter) details a new four-person Section 925(c) restoration batch the DOJ announced Thursday, backdated to former AG Pam Bondi’s March 30 grants. One recipient, Wayne Gunther Heussman — prohibited after a 2007 misdemeanor DUI — will drop his pending 2A lawsuit once the restoration takes effect. The standardized-process rule that drew 3,427 comments remains unpublished; DOJ has leaned on the promise of that rule to steer courts (and SCOTUS) away from prohibited-person challenges, so a stalled rule has knock-on effects for several strong test cases. The likeliest cause is the ordinary slog of processing comments — the timeline hasn’t yet slipped past normal — but it’s worth watching.

Also live this morning. The Reload podcast on alleged racial discrimination in New Jersey’s permitting system (Slate’s Aymann Ismail; a tracked 10-to-1 Black-vs-white denial disparity for applicants without disqualifying records) goes public to non-members today. New York’s budget-bill digital-gun-file / 3D-printer / Glock ban (A10005c) still awaits the signaled NRA/FPC/SAF suit. The 2026 record-low-murder-rate story (AH Datalytics) carries forward. Colorado’s SB25-003 draft-list comment window is closing (~today); CO Initiative 89’s signature deadline passed Saturday (outcome unverified). SCOTUS Wolford v. Lopez decision still pending by end of June.

What this means for RTB content posture today. The reciprocity story is a clean, empowerment-led explainer: why your carry permit doesn’t automatically travel, what reciprocity actually is, and why the path now runs through Congress, not the courtroom. It pairs naturally with RTB’s legal-coverage value proposition for members who travel across state lines. The rights-restoration thread is a careful Section 925(c) explainer (what it is, who qualifies, why the delay matters) — factual, no overpromising. Keep anchoring the murder-rate data to the “coverage for the world as it actually is” frame. Let the facts carry it.

Moved ▲ Courts · 8th Cir. Heat84▲▲▲ June 1 · The Reload (Fogleman), CA8

Eighth Circuit Johnson v. Jacobson — reciprocity dismissal becomes the weekend’s defining analysis

The unanimous panel (Judge L. Steven Grasz writing) dismissed a challenge to Minnesota’s recognition of only 33 states’ carry permits, holding the reciprocity statute “standing alone, does not regulate arms-bearing conduct” and so fails at Bruen step one. Because Minnesota offers shall-issue permits to residents and non-residents alike, the court found reciprocity is “interstate comity, not the Second Amendment.” Fogleman’s analysis pairs it with CRPA v. LASD (Judge Garnett rejecting an Obergefell analogy) to show even well-briefed reciprocity claims lose on the same logic. SAF’s Kostas Moros called the result unsurprising given thin “traveler’s exceptions” briefing. A Liberty Justice Center cert-petition decision is the next watch.

RTB angle: This is the week’s cleanest legal-literacy moment: explain the difference between winning the right to a non-resident permit (a real, won fight) and forcing reciprocity (a much harder one), and why that pushes the issue to Congress. Empowerment through understanding.

Moved ▲ Regulatory · DOJ Heat80→ May 29 · The Reload (Gutowski), Federal Register

DOJ Sec. 925(c) rights-restoration rule — still missing; four-person batch backdated to ex-AG Bondi

DOJ announced a new batch of federal firearms-disability restorations — just four individuals, backdated to former AG Pam Bondi’s March 30 grants (Principal Associate Deputy AG Trent McCotter signed the notice). It offered no update on the standardized rights-restoration process whose comment period closed seven months ago after 3,427 comments. One recipient, Wayne Gunther Heussman (prohibited after a 2007 misdemeanor DUI), plans to drop his Second Amendment lawsuit once the restoration takes effect. DOJ has repeatedly invoked the promise of a formal rule to discourage courts — and the Supreme Court — from taking up prohibited-person challenges, so a stalled rule has downstream effects. The likeliest cause is the ordinary work of processing comments; the timeline hasn’t yet slipped past typical.

RTB angle: A careful Section 925(c) explainer: what restoration is, who is eligible (DOJ estimates potentially millions), why a standardized process matters, and why the delay ripples into pending 2A cases. Factual and measured — no overpromising on outcomes or timelines.
