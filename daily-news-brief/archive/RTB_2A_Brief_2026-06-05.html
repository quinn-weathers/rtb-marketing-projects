Friday open: Virginia’s background-check standoff breaks — the Lynchburg court kept the injunction in place and the State Police stood down. The same news cycle delivered May NICS data showing Virginia gun sales more than doubled, a verified prosecutor-refusal count of 14, a 9th Circuit ruling that suppressors aren’t Second Amendment “arms,” and an NRA shake-up with $176 million on the line.

Virginia: the injunction stands, and enforcement stops. Per AmmoLand’s detailed report, the Lynchburg judge on Wednesday June 3 firmly rejected the request from Attorney General Jay Jones and the Virginia State Police to dissolve the standing injunction against universal background checks on private sales — affirming the injunction is fully in place and emphasizing that new legislation cannot unilaterally supersede a court’s constitutional ruling. Following the decision, the VSP agreed to cease conducting private-sale checks and committed to updating its website. The disposition of VCDL/GOA’s parallel contempt motion was not detailed in available reporting, and The Reload had not yet published its own account of the ruling as of this morning — treat granular details as single-source until cross-confirmed. The practical ambiguity that hung over Virginia private transfers for nine days is, for now, resolved: the pre-injunction status quo is back.

The May numbers landed — and they are a Virginia story. NSSF’s adjusted NICS figures posted this week: 1,105,758 sales-related checks nationally, up 3.2% year-over-year. Virginia processed 74,959 checks — a 103% increase over last May — ranking fourth overall, second in rifle/shotgun checks behind Texas, and second in NFA checks (12,550) amid the nationwide silencer-sales jump following the federal tax repeal. NSSF’s Bartozzi called the surge “cause and effect” ahead of the July 1 ban. And the prosecutor-refusal count is no longer an op-ed claim: VCDL announced Tuesday that 14 Commonwealth’s Attorneys have now said they won’t enforce the hardware bans, with Page and Floyd counties the newest — The Reload reports the figure on the news side, resolving Wednesday’s “ten (Unverified)” item upward.

Courts: a suppressor setback, and no Wolford yet. The 9th Circuit ruled in United States v. DeBorba — a bad-facts criminal case — that suppressors are not “arms” protected by the Second Amendment’s plain text, language that may now reach the cleaner California ban challenge (Sanchez v. Bonta) already argued and awaiting decision. Within 48 hours of The Reload’s footnote analysis, that’s a second strong signal that suppressor deregulation runs through Congress, not the courts. At SCOTUS, Thursday’s opinions came and went without Wolford v. Lopez; 23 decisions remain. Next checkpoints: the orders list Monday June 8 (first cert-watch window from Thursday’s conference) and opinions again Thursday June 11.

NRA: appeal lost, Foundation walks. A New York appellate panel unanimously upheld the verdict requiring Wayne LaPierre to repay the NRA about $4.3 million and barring him from affiliate leadership for four years — his lawyer vows further appeal. The same day, the LaPierre-aligned NRA Foundation announced it will split from the NRA entirely and rename itself the 1791 Foundation. The Foundation holds roughly $176 million — nearly 70% of the organization’s net assets — making this the most consequential gun-group governance story of the year.

What this means for RTB content posture today. The Virginia “what we know / what changed” piece gets its green light — there is finally an outcome to report: the injunction stands, private-sale checks are off again, and an appeal remains possible. Publish calm and source-linked. The suppressor legal-literacy explainer should be refreshed with DeBorba — it makes the “why this only moves in Congress” point concrete. The May NICS note writes itself as evidence-led demand context. The Wolford explainer re-holsters until Thursday’s window. The NRA story is context, not content — track, don’t editorialize.

New ▲ Courts · 9th Circuit Heat76▲ June 4 · AmmoLand, 9th Cir. opinion

9th Circuit rules suppressors are not Second Amendment “arms” (U.S. v. DeBorba) — bad-facts case, broad language

Upholding federal convictions of a defendant who was unlawfully present, claimed citizenship on firearms paperwork, was subject to DV no-contact orders, and possessed an unregistered suppressor, the panel went further: suppressors are accessories “not necessary to the ordinary operation of a firearm” and thus outside the Second Amendment’s plain text. The timing concern is concrete — Sanchez v. Bonta, the cleaner challenge to California’s total suppressor ban, has already been argued in the same circuit, and commentators (Kostas Moros, Mark W. Smith) warn DeBorba could foreclose its arguments before decision. Together with the SCOTUS-footnote pattern The Reload documented Wednesday, the courts’ door on suppressor deregulation looks firmly shut.
