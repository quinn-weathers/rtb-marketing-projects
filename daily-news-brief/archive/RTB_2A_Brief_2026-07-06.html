Top of mind07 · 06

The AR-15 case has taken over the conversation. Over the holiday weekend the entire firearms-policy world turned to Viramontes v. Cook County — and a cross-ideological group of Second Amendment scholars told The Reload they expect the Supreme Court to strike down the AR-15 “assault weapon” bans. The open questions now are how broadly the Court rules and what the justices reveal at fall oral argument. Back home, Virginia’s ban sits in a “muddled” limbo — with a consolidation hearing set for today, Monday, July 6.

The expert consensus: the bans are likely to fall. A group of scholars from across the ideological spectrum — David Kopel (Independence Institute), Andrew Willinger (Georgia State), Josh Blackman (Cato / South Texas), Hayley Lawrence (Duke Center for Firearms Law), F. Lee Francis (Widener), George Mocsary (Wyoming Firearms Research Center) and Eric Ruben (SMU) — told The Reload the Court is likely to tip toward the plaintiffs, though some were far more confident than others (Mocsary and Ruben declined to predict). The engine is Heller’s “common use” test: AR-15-platform rifles are among the most commonly owned firearms in the country — owned by millions, likely tens of millions — and this term’s Wolford (6–3 carry win) and Hemani (9–0) decisions tightened Bruen’s “why and how” analogue discipline in ways the experts read as tailwinds for the challengers. Cross-confirmed: The Reload’s July 3 scholar survey.

The live debate is breadth, not outcome. The two votes to watch are Chief Justice Roberts and Justice Barrett — the Wolford-majority justices without a known position on AR-15 bans. Experts expect them to reach the same result as the other Republican appointees but to split on how far to go: a sweeping categorical “common-use” holding versus a narrow, statute-specific ruling or even a remand (Francis flags Barrett’s caution on “level of generality”). Thomas and Kavanaugh — per their statements in Snope v. Brown — plus Gorsuch and Alito are already read as skeptical of the bans.

What scholars will listen for at oral argument (this fall). Whether the Solicitor General’s office argues for the petitioners (as it did in Wolford); whether the Court explains why it took a case with no circuit split (every appeals court to review an AR-15 ban has upheld it); and, above all, how the justices handle “common use” — where they place it in the test, how they measure “common,” and where they draw the “dangerous and unusual” line. A key subplot: the Court took a firearms-only case, leaving the magazine-ban question for another day.

Virginia — still muddled, hearing today. The “assault firearms” ban took effect July 1 but into a partial-injunction “muddled mess”: the NRA’s June 29 statewide injunction binds only the named defendants (the State Police and six Commonwealth’s Attorneys), the Attorney General says the law “remains in force” against everyone else, and retailers statewide pulled AR-15s rather than risk liability. A hearing on the AG’s motion to consolidate the challenges and move venue is set for today, Monday, July 6 — the next procedural hinge (the NSSF-backed suit is on hold pending it). Its outcome is not yet reported; we carry it as developing.
