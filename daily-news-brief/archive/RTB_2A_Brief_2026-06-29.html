This is the Supreme Court’s last decision day of the term — and the long-relisted 2A petitions are still on the table. Orders are expected at 9:30 a.m. ET and the final opinions at 10 a.m. ET, with eight argued cases still undecided. As of this 6:00 ET refresh the orders list has not yet posted, so the fate of the magazine and AR-15 petitions is still open (Unverified). Two more hard dates land this week: DOJ–California tomorrow (Jun 30, now T-1) and Virginia’s two-track July 1.

The headline: it’s decision day, and the cert cluster is the open question. After Wolford and Hemani came down last week, a stack of long-relisted Second Amendment petitions remains undecided — Duncan v. Bonta (California’s magazine cap) and Gator’s Custom Guns v. Washington (each relisted roughly 20 times), plus the AR-15 “assault weapon” petitions (Viramontes v. Cook County, NAGR v. Lamont, Grant v. Higgins). SCOTUSblog is live-blogging today’s 9:30 a.m. orders and 10 a.m. opinions. A grant, a denial with a written dissent, or yet another hold are all live possibilities — we publish the decoder, not a prediction, once the list posts.

What last week actually settled — and didn’t. The term produced the most Second Amendment opinions in a single sitting: Wolford struck Hawaii’s “carry only with consent” default 6–3 (reaching CA, MD, NY & NJ), and Hemani unanimously rejected automatic disarmament based solely on marijuana use. But The Reload’s term-wrap analysis stresses that both rulings were written narrowly: the Court again declined to resolve the 1791-vs-1868 history question (Barrett concurrence in Wolford) and expressly left the felon-in-possession and other prohibited-person questions open (Gorsuch in Hemani). The practical read: lower courts inclined to uphold restrictions still have room to do so until the Court reverses them directly — which is exactly why today’s cert decisions matter.

The Wolford ripple is already moving in the AWB fight. Plaintiffs challenging New Jersey’s semi-auto ban have filed a Rule 28(j) letter arguing Wolford reinforces that “Arms” are protected at Bruen’s plain-text step — i.e., that courts can’t define common firearms out of the Second Amendment before reaching history and tradition. Expect similar filings in the Virginia, Illinois and Maryland cases.

And the federal front stays live. The DOJ’s pre-suit letter gives California until 5:00 p.m. ET June 30 (now T-1) to negotiate over the AB 1127 “Glock ban” and handgun roster or face a complaint. In Virginia, July 1 arrives Wednesday: the SB749 hardware ban is enjoined statewide through Dec. 31 (Crump, appeal expected), while universal background checks return (Wilson).

S. 218 (Hearing Protection Act) — Comeaux’s “silencers are Arms” holding keeps the suppressor question alive, but registration stands; Congress remains the dereg path

The Fifth Circuit’s Comeaux decision still frames the suppressor fight as the Court closes its term: a federal appeals court squarely held that suppressors are Second Amendment “Arms,” rejecting DOJ’s contrary position — yet upheld the NFA’s registration requirement under the circuit’s Peterson footnote-9 presumption. Virginia “assault firearms” ban (SB749) stays BLOCKED statewide through Dec. 31 as July 1 nears — watch for the Commonwealth’s emergency appeal

Two days out from the July 1 effective date, the statewide preliminary injunction from Crump v. Katz holds: Lancaster County Circuit Judge John Martin enjoined enforcement of SB 749 until Dec. 31, refused to limit relief to one county, and refused to stay his own order. AG Jay Jones (D.) has said he will “urgently file a motion to stay” and appeal, so an appellate court could still move before Wednesday. The court reached the injunction through individual plaintiff John Crump (an AmmoLand contributor), holding that GOA and VCDL lacked standing; Crump rests on Virginia’s own constitution (Art. I, §13). Separately, the broader Washington County suit (Sen. Bill Stanley) seeking relief against all law enforcement — not just the State Police — is still awaiting a ruling. This is preliminary, not a merits decision.

Virginia universal background checks set to return July 1 — the dissolved Wilson v. Katz injunction takes effect in two days

On the other track, a Lynchburg court dissolved the permanent injunction that had blocked Virginia’s universal-background-check mandate for private sales. Unless an appellate court intervenes, Virginia State Police may resume processing private-transfer checks Wednesday, July 1. The case (formerly Wilson v. Hanley) was brought by GOA, the Gun Owners Foundation, the VCDL and individual plaintiffs. In October 2025 the court had struck the law entirely — because the federal check system created a de facto handgun-purchase ban for 18-to-20-year-olds — and enjoined it statewide. Virginia responded with HB 1525, raising the minimum handgun- and “assault firearm”-purchase age to 21 (signed with an emergency clause April 22); the AG then argued the fix mooted the injunction. GOF called the mandate a potential “backdoor gun registry” and vowed to appeal.

RTB angle: Pair this with Crump as a single “what actually changes in Virginia on July 1” explainer — the hardware ban is paused, but private-sale background checks return. Practical, counter-level clarity for members; note the procedural posture (an appeal is expected) and avoid alarm.

After Hemani: the 9–0 decision’s due-process language becomes the new argument against red-flag laws, with Thomas & Jackson staking out the poles

More than a week after the unanimous Hemani judgment (rejecting automatic disarmament based solely on regular marijuana use), the commentary continues to shape how the decision gets used. Analysts argue its emphasis on process gives gun-rights litigants a fresh tool against red-flag laws that “seize first and offer a hearing later.” The concurrences frame the next fights: Justice Thomas questioned whether Congress can criminalize purely intrastate possession under §922(g) at all (a commerce-clause challenge), while Justice Jackson — concurring in the judgment — called Bruen a “failed experiment” and urged a return to means-end scrutiny. The throughline: Hemani’s history-and-tradition discipline is being read as the lever for the next round of as-applied challenges.
