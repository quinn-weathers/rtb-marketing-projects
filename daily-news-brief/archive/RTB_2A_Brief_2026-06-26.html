Top of mind06 · 26

The lone pending 2A case is decided — and it’s a win. The Supreme Court struck Hawaii’s “carry only with the owner’s consent” default 6–3, with ripple effects in California, Maryland, New York, and New Jersey. The same week, Virginia’s “assault firearms” ban was blocked statewide through Dec. 31 — while the state’s universal-background-check mandate is set to return July 1. Three hard dates now cluster: orders Mon Jun 29, DOJ–California Jun 30, and July 1 (VA, CO).

The headline: Wolford v. Lopez is decided. By a vote of 6–3, the Court held that Hawaii’s 2023 law — which made it a crime to carry on private property open to the public absent the owner’s express consent — violates the Second Amendment. Writing for the majority, Justice Alito said the law “hobbles what the Second Amendment protects: the right of Americans to carry arms for self-defense as they go about their daily lives,” and found the state’s historical analogues (deer-poaching statutes; an 1865 Louisiana plantation-carry law) “just too wide” a gap. Justice Jackson, joined by Justice Sotomayor, dissented, arguing the law “fairly applies a first principle of property law—the right to exclude.” The decision reaches the four other states with similar “default-no-carry” private-property rules: California, Maryland, New York, and New Jersey.

Virginia, two rulings, opposite directions. In Crump v. Katz, a state judge issued a statewide preliminary injunction blocking Virginia’s new “assault firearms” ban (SB 749) through Dec. 31 — refused to limit relief to one county, and refused to stay his own order. Individual plaintiff and AmmoLand contributor John Crump was found to have standing; GOA and VCDL were not. Separately, in Wilson v. Katz, a Lynchburg court dissolved the injunction that had blocked Virginia’s universal-background-check mandate, so UBC enforcement is set to resume July 1 after the legislature raised the handgun-purchase age to 21 to cure the defect the court had identified. Both rulings are expected to draw appeals.

At the Court, the cert cluster is now front and center. With Wolford resolved, attention turns to the term’s final conference and the long-relisted Second Amendment petitions — Duncan v. Bonta (California magazine cap) and Gator’s Custom Guns v. Washington (both at their 20th relist), plus the AR-15 “assault weapon” petitions (Viramontes, NAGR v. Lamont, Grant v. Higgins). SCOTUSblog expects the last argued-case opinions next week and a likely “mop-up” conference; orders are expected Monday, June 29. A grant, a deny-with-dissent, or another hold all read as major.

And the federal front stays live. The DOJ’s pre-suit letter gives California until 5:00 p.m. ET June 30 to negotiate over the AB 1127 “Glock ban” and handgun roster or face a complaint (T-4).

Virginia “assault firearms” ban (SB749) BLOCKED statewide through Dec. 31 — judge in Crump v. Katz refuses a county-only limit and refuses to stay his own order

Days before the July 1 effective date, a Virginia state judge read a statewide preliminary injunction from the bench halting enforcement of SB 749 until Dec. 31 while the case proceeds. The Commonwealth asked the court to limit relief to Lancaster County — the judge refused. The state then asked him to stay his own ruling — he refused that too. The court narrowed the case first, holding that Gun Owners of America and the Virginia Citizens Defense League lacked standing but that individual plaintiff John Crump (an AmmoLand contributor) did, which was enough to reach the injunction. Crump rests on Virginia’s own constitution (Art. I, §13); the state argued that provision protects only a collective, militia-tethered right — i.e., that Virginians have less protection under their constitution than under the federal Bill of Rights. This is a preliminary ruling, not a merits decision, and the Commonwealth can seek emergency appellate relief; expect an appeal.

New ▲ SCOTUS · Merits Heat96▲ June 26 · SCOTUSblog (Howe), slip opinion

DECIDED: Supreme Court strikes Hawaii’s “carry only with consent” default 6–3 in Wolford v. Lopez — reaches CA, MD, NY & NJ

The Court held that Hawaii’s 2023 law — which made it a crime to carry a firearm onto private property open to the public unless the owner gives specific consent — violates the Second Amendment. In a 24-page opinion, Justice Alito wrote that the law “hobbles what the Second Amendment protects: the right of Americans to carry arms for self-defense as they go about their daily lives.” He found the state’s historical analogues insufficient: colonial deer/game anti-poaching statutes left “just too wide” a gap, and an 1865 Louisiana plantation-carry statute was “neither widespread nor widely accepted” and was adopted to discriminate against formerly enslaved people. Justice Jackson, joined by Justice Sotomayor, dissented, arguing the law “fairly applies a first principle of property law—the right to exclude” and does no harm to the Second Amendment. The ruling directly affects four other states with similar default-no-carry rules for private property open to the public: California, Maryland, New York, and New Jersey. The challenge was brought by three Maui concealed-carry permit holders and a local gun-rights group; the Ninth Circuit had upheld the law before the Court reversed.
