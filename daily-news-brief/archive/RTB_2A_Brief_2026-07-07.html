Virginia’s ban challengers just won a procedural round. On Monday, July 6 a three-judge panel refused to consolidate the four lawsuits against the Commonwealth’s gun-control laws — so each case now proceeds in the court where it was filed, and Attorney General Jay Jones does not get the single, friendlier forum he asked for. The enforcement picture is still “muddled,” the AR-15 case (Viramontes) still frames the national debate, and the DOJ’s California “Glock ban” fight is now confirmed and live.

Virginia — consolidation denied. A three-judge panel denied the Commonwealth’s application to transfer and combine Santolla v. Katz, Crump v. Katz, Curtis v. Katz, and Black v. Hook into one proceeding. The panel found that common questions of law or fact do not predominate, that the suits rest on different statutory frameworks and legal theories, and that they are already too far along — three of the four have preliminary-injunction rulings — so, in the panel’s words, “it is too late to properly prevent inconsistent rulings.” It also rejected the state’s “convenience” argument. Cross-confirmed: AmmoLand’s report and a Gun Owners of America statement (July 6). This resolves the “developing” hearing we carried yesterday.

What it means — and what it doesn’t. This is not a ruling on whether the bans are constitutional; it is a procedural setback for the state’s defense strategy. The four suits now advance separately, each on its own theory: statutory-interpretation and public-carry questions in Crump and Santolla, a federal Second Amendment / right-to-hunt / vagueness challenge in Black v. Hook, and the Virginia Constitution’s militia clause in Curtis. The broader “muddled” enforcement situation is unchanged: the NRA’s June 29 statewide injunction still binds only the named defendants (State Police + six Commonwealth’s Attorneys), the AG maintains the law “remains in force” against everyone else, and retailers statewide are still holding AR-15s off the shelf.

AR-15 case still the backdrop. Viramontes v. Cook County (25-238, consolidated with Grant v. Higgins, 25-566) remains in its analysis phase after last week’s cross-ideological scholar survey (The Reload) predicting the Court is likely to strike the bans, with breadth — not outcome — the live question and Roberts & Barrett the votes to watch. A fresh July 6 analysis frames the grant as “the beginning of the end” for AR-15 bans under Bruen. Oral argument is expected this fall.

California — DOJ “Glock ban” suit now confirmed (correction). We carried this as Unverified; it is now confirmed. On July 1 the DOJ filed United States v. California & Bonta, challenging AB 1127 (the ban on “machine-gun-convertible” Glock-pattern pistols) as facially unconstitutional under Heller’s common-use rule, and sought an ex-parte temporary restraining order. California opposed the TRO on July 3, arguing Glocks fail Bruen step one, that there is “no constitutional right to factory settings,” and that the pistols are “dangerous and unusual.” A second Glock-ban challenge, NRA v. Moore, is pending against Maryland.

Verification note. The Virginia consolidation denial is cross-confirmed (AmmoLand + Gun Owners of America); the DOJ–California posture is now confirmed from the filings. Still Unverified this run: whether Virginia has yet filed AG Jones’s promised emergency stay/appeal of the injunctions; the ruling on DOJ’s California TRO; and Colorado’s SB25-003 final “Specified Semiautomatic Firearms” list contents and the Initiative 89 signature outcome.

New ▲ 7th Circuit · Mental-health ban Heat68▲ July 7 · AmmoLand (M. W. Smith)

U.S. v. Rose — the Seventh Circuit’s Judge Easterbrook holds the lifetime mental-health gun ban may be invalid without proof of present danger, vacating and remanding

In an opinion by Judge Frank Easterbrook — a 1985 Reagan appointee with a gun-control-friendly record who joined Bevis v. Naperville upholding Illinois’s rifle ban — the Seventh Circuit held that 18 U.S.C. §922(g)(4), which bars anyone ever committed to a mental institution, may be unconstitutional as applied to someone no longer mentally ill or dangerous. The court vacated the dismissal of charges against Jonathan Rose (involuntarily committed in 2009, released in 2010, never recommitted) and remanded for fact-finding on present dangerousness. Easterbrook wrote that Heller’s assurance about “the mentally ill” is present-tense and “not people who used to be mentally ill,” and that the government’s contrary position is “hard to square with Heller, McDonald, Bruen, Rahimi, Hemani, and Wolford.” This is a remand, not a win for Rose — but the rule it embraces (dangerousness assessed as of now, not from a decades-old record) is the story.
