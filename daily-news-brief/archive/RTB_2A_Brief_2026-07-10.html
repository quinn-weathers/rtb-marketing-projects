Top of mind07 · 10

The Seventh Circuit just upheld Illinois’ AR-15 ban — days after SCOTUS agreed to decide the exact same question. In Barnett v. Raoul, a divided panel reversed the trial-court injunction and doubled down on Bevis, with Chief Judge Brennan dissenting that Illinois has banned “the best-selling rifle in America and its standard magazine.” The circuit has answered; now it’s SCOTUS’ turn.

What the court did. A divided Seventh Circuit panel — Judge Amy St. Eve writing, joined by Judge Frank Easterbrook — reversed Judge Stephen McGlynn’s permanent injunction against the Protect Illinois Communities Act, leaving Illinois free to keep enforcing its ban on AR-15-pattern rifles and standard 30-round magazines. The majority leaned on the circuit’s earlier Bevis reasoning (AR-15s as “close enough” to M16s to sit outside full protection), read Rahimi as rejecting any need for exact historical twins, and cited a lineup of other circuits that have upheld similar bans. Chief Judge Michael Brennan dissented, warning that after “perhaps the most comprehensive trial record in any Second Amendment case to date” the court simply repeated its Bevis error. (Single-source this run: AmmoLand’s report, which is openly opinion-inflected; the ruling itself is concrete but The Reload had not yet covered it — treat characterizations as the author’s.)

Why it matters. The timing is the story: SCOTUS granted cert in Viramontes/Grant on June 30 to decide whether the Second Amendment protects common semiautomatic rifles — and the Seventh Circuit answered “no” anyway, days later. Justice Thomas had previously written that if the circuit ultimately let Illinois ban “America’s most common civilian rifle,” the Court “can—and should—review that decision.” Barnett now reads as the circuit’s final defense of Bevis before the question is taken out of its hands — and it sharpens the fall argument’s stakes in both directions. For Illinois owners, nothing improves today; the ban stays enforceable there while Virginia’s heads the other way.

The second story: Virginia’s statewide injunction is now cross-confirmed — and the market is already moving. The Reload’s July 8 report confirms Judge Campbell’s Santolla v. Katz order (released July 8, official July 21): every police officer and prosecutor in the Commonwealth is barred from enforcing the “assault firearms”/magazine bans, with Campbell distinguishing the federal universal-injunction ban on the ground that Virginia law authorizes statewide relief. New details: Palmetto State Armory declared “Virginia is back open” and is accepting Virginia orders now (held for shipment until July 21); NSSF says guidance to member businesses is coming; lawmakers separately voted to delay the companion carry ban; and AG Jones has already appealed the Crump injunction to the Virginia Supreme Court. The Reload’s new analysis asks whether that court will simply wait for SCOTUS — under DiGiacinto, Virginia’s arms right is read co-extensively with the Second Amendment, so Viramontes may effectively decide the state question too.
