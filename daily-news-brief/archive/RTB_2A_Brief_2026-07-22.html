The hardware-ban map is being redrawn with dates attached: New Jersey’s own State Police now tell dealers the Third Circuit ruling takes effect July 31 — a week after Virginia’s injunction went live and the same week a gun-rights coalition moved to block Maryland’s Glock ban before it can ever start.

What’s new. The New Jersey State Police Firearms Dealer Investigation Unit has emailed FFLs confirming that the en banc Third Circuit’s decision in ANJRPC v. Platkin — striking the state’s “assault firearm” rifle ban and its 10-round magazine limit — “is effective July 31, 2026,” and that the agency is “working with the Office of the Attorney General to develop practical guidance.” That is a notable posture from the body that enforces New Jersey’s gun laws: the state may dislike the ruling, but its police are preparing dealers for it to take hold. The precision that matters for members mirrors Virginia’s: dealers such as Green Brook’s Middlebrook Firearms say they will keep complying with existing restrictions until the effective date — the email is not permission to disregard current statutes today — and the notice does not yet explain how post-July-31 transfers, previously banned rifles, or 11-plus-round magazines will be handled. The remanded pistol/shotgun questions are still in district court, and the state has not said whether it will seek further relief before July 31. (Primary-source-based: AmmoLand reporting the NJSP email and dealer post; mainstream and Reload corroboration not yet available this run.)

The second front: Maryland’s Glock ban gets challenged before it starts. On July 20 the Second Amendment Foundation, NRA, Firearms Policy Coalition, dealer Atlantic Guns, and two residents moved for a preliminary injunction in Bathras v. Moore, asking a federal judge to block Senate Bill 334 before its handgun restrictions become enforceable January 1, 2027. SB 334 targets “machine-gun convertible pistols” — a definition the plaintiffs say sweeps in factory-stock Glocks and Glock-pattern pistols that use a cruciform trigger bar, even though switches and auto-sears are already illegal under state and federal law. The coalition turns Maryland’s own Bianchi win against it: the Fourth Circuit called the Glock 19 a “commonly used handgun” when upholding the rifle ban. No court has ruled the law unconstitutional; this is a motion, not a decision. (Single-source: AmmoLand, quoting the motion and SAF.)

The third thread: Virginia, Day 1. A week after Judge Campbell’s Santolla v. Katz injunction went operative, covered-rifle and magazine sales are underway across the Commonwealth with no reported day-of disruption; the statute remains enjoined rather than repealed, the carry ban stays separately delayed, and the Commonwealth’s appeal — anchored by the AG’s Crump “not protected arms” motion — is the caveat on everything. Three states, three clocks: Virginia live, New Jersey July 31, Maryland fighting a 2027 start date in court now.

U.S. v. Squire, Day 2: the en banc Fifth Circuit’s vacatur reframes §922(g)(1) as a facial Commerce Clause challenge — “already, our court answers the call”

The story holds as the docket text is awaited. Three weeks after Justice Thomas’s Hemani concurrence asked lower courts to reexamine whether the Commerce Clause supports §922(g), the full Fifth Circuit vacated a panel opinion upholding Curtis Squire’s felon-in-possession conviction and took the question en banc; Judge Higginson’s dissent supplies the framing (“what was a Second Amendment as-applied challenge… a facial Commerce Clause challenge”). The theory under review is the minimal nexus: a gun that once crossed a state line supporting federal jurisdiction over purely local possession forever. The facts are unsympathetic by design (Squire’s record includes trafficking and burglary), which is what makes it a clean test of the principle. What hasn’t happened: no statute struck, no conviction reversed, no timeline set. A government loss would implicate every §922(g) category in TX/LA/MS and split the circuits instantly. (Single-source: AmmoLand; the order and briefing schedule are checkable on the CA5 docket — still owed.)

New ▲ D. Virgin Islands · Nonresident carry Heat62▲▲▲July 20 · AmmoLand (complaint + GOA; single-source)

GOA sues the U.S. Virgin Islands over a nonresident carry blockade — Henry v. Brooks argues the Second Amendment “doesn’t end at the shoreline”

Gun Owners of America, Gun Owners Foundation, and Florida resident Sean Dale Henry filed Henry v. Brooks on July 20 in the U.S. District Court for the Virgin Islands, challenging a licensing scheme that, per the complaint, gives ordinary American visitors no lawful path to possess or carry a defensive handgun: the territory requires a license, recognizes no out-of-state permits, generally bars nonresidents from applying, prohibits open carry, and imposes a ten-year mandatory minimum (14 V.I.C. §2253) for unauthorized possession — a floor as severe as the federal maximum for illegal machine-gun possession. The complaint says the USVI repealed its limited reciprocity law as part of a post-Bruen “response bill.” GOA frames it as the next step in its nonresident-carry campaign after Higbie v. James (N.Y., 2025); it is not seeking to eliminate licensing, only to force the territory to accept qualified nonresident applications. The government has not yet answered. (Single-source: AmmoLand, quoting the complaint and GOA’s Luis Valdes.)
