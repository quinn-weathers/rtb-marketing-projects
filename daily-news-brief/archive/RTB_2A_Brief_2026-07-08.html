The mental-health gun-ban ruling is now cross-confirmed — and it’s narrower than it first read. On Monday evening The Reload confirmed the Seventh Circuit’s decision in U.S. v. Rose, and the details sharpen the story: the court upheld the lifetime ban (§922(g)(4)) as facially constitutional, but left it open to as-applied challenges — and it vacated the dismissal that had favored the defendant. A quiet summer-recess day otherwise: the AR-15 case, Virginia’s four suits, and the DOJ’s California “Glock ban” fight all roll forward.

What the Seventh Circuit actually held. In a unanimous panel opinion by Judge Frank Easterbrook (decided July 2, No. 24-1086), the court held that 18 U.S.C. §922(g)(4) — which bars gun possession by anyone ever committed to a mental institution — is facially constitutional, but potentially vulnerable to as-applied challenges for someone whose condition was transitory or who was committed in error. Because “every relevant historical practice disarmed a mentally ill person only during civil commitment or ongoing insanity,” a lifetime bar can’t automatically reach a person who is not dangerous today. Crucially, this is not a win for Jonathan Rose: the panel vacated the district court’s dismissal of his charges and remanded for fact-finding, listing seven questions (why he was committed in 2009, whether he recovered, his current treatment, his disability-benefits representations) on which the government may still supply “individualized proof” of present danger. Cross-confirmed: The Reload (July 7) + AmmoLand (carried July 7).

Why it matters — and the correction. We carried this July 7 as the ban “may be invalid”; the more precise reading is that the statute stands on its face and the fight moves to individual, present-danger records. The principle — dangerousness assessed as of now, not from a decades-old file — is the same Rahimi/Hemani throughline RTB has tracked, and it dovetails with the revived §925(c) rights-restoration process. It’s incremental, case-by-case doctrine, not a sweeping change — exactly the calm, clarifying lane RTB occupies. Sensitive-topic framing: handle mental-health content with care and neutrality.

Rolling forward. Virginia’s four “assault firearms” suits (Santolla, Crump, Curtis, Black v. Hook) continue separately after Monday’s consolidation denial; the DOJ’s California AB 1127 “Glock ban” suit is confirmed and awaiting a TRO ruling; and the SCOTUS AR-15 merits case, Viramontes v. Cook County, remains in its analysis phase with fall argument expected.

Texas Senate: Talarico calls himself a 2A “believer” but backs background checks, storage, and age limits — Paxton race a dead heat

Democratic nominee James Talarico said he believes in the Second Amendment “just as much as” the First but that “like any freedom… it’s not absolute,” committing to universal background checks and safe-storage laws with trafficking-prosecution priorities and age restrictions on certain magazines and “assault weapons,” while declining to say whether he’d back a full sales ban. He pitched a “middle path,” criticizing both gun-confiscation Democrats and the NRA. The race against Republican AG Ken Paxton — polling as a dead heat (Siena) — could be a tipping point for Senate control.
