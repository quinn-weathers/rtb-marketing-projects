Three circuits, three answers in a single month: the First Circuit’s decision upholding Massachusetts’ AR-15 ban completes the appellate split the Supreme Court will resolve this fall — and the Justice Department has opened a second front in the Virgin Islands.

Sourcing status — live access restored. After several roll-forward editions, this run reached The Reload, AmmoLand, NSSF, and SCOTUSblog directly. Items below are freshly reported and dated; where a development rests on a single outlet or an un-reviewed filing, it is marked. Cross-confirmed today: the Third Circuit’s New Jersey ruling and Virginia’s live sales (two outlets each). Primary-source-anchored: the First Circuit opinion (quoted at length by The Reload) and the DOJ Virgin Islands motion (quoted by AmmoLand).

The lead. On Tuesday, July 21, a unanimous First Circuit panel in Recchia v. Campbell declined to revive a challenge to Massachusetts’ ban on “assault-style” rifles, holding that its 2024 precedent Capen v. Campbell controlled and that Heller itself forecloses the argument that the Second Amendment guarantees a right to arms “equivalent to those carried by the modern-day soldier.” It is the third federal appellate ruling on a state “assault weapon” ban this month alone: the Seventh Circuit upheld Illinois’, the en banc Third Circuit struck New Jersey’s, and now the First upholds Massachusetts’. That is precisely the entrenched split the Court agreed to resolve in Viramontes and Grant next term — and most legal observers still expect the Court to reach the bans skeptically.

The second new front. On July 22 the Justice Department asked a federal judge to block core provisions of the U.S. Virgin Islands’ new Act 9113 — a 15-round magazine cap and a bar on transferring firearms “manufactured to hold” more than 15 rounds — arguing the Third Circuit’s Cheeseman (now ANJRPC v. Attorney General) already settles the question because the territory sits within the Third Circuit. “ANJRPC squarely holds that this is unconstitutional,” DOJ wrote. The motion rides an existing DOJ suit against the territory; the judge has set argument for August 19.

The clocks worth pre-positioning for. New Jersey’s July 31 effective date is now seven days out (T-7), Colorado’s permit-to-purchase regime begins Aug 1 (T-8), the ATF “engaged in the business” comment deadline is Aug 4 (T-11) alongside NSSF’s Import/Export Conference, National Shooting Sports Month opens Aug 1, and the next Supreme Court order list is Aug 17. The empowerment lane is unchanged: precise, calm countdowns and “comply until the date” discipline — the map, not the cheerleading.

Decided ▲ 1st Circuit · AR-15 ban Heat86▲▲The Reload (opinion quoted at length; primary PDF linked)

Recchia v. Campbell: a unanimous First Circuit upholds Massachusetts’ “assault weapon” ban — the month’s third appellate answer, and the split now before the Supreme Court

On Tuesday, July 21, a three-judge First Circuit panel unanimously declined to revive a challenge to Massachusetts’ ban on the sale of “assault-style firearms,” holding that its 2024 precedent Capen v. Campbell controlled. Judge Seth Aframe (a Biden appointee), joined by Judges Sandra Lynch (Clinton) and Joshua Dunlap (Trump), wrote that plaintiff Gino Recchia — a gun-store owner — “presented no persuasive argument for why this analysis should not control.” The panel also rejected Recchia’s newer theory that the Second Amendment protects “weapons equivalent to those carried by the modern-day soldier,” finding it foreclosed by Heller’s own passage acknowledging that “weapons that are most useful in military service — M-16 rifles and the like — may be banned.” The Reload notes this is the third appellate ruling on a state ban this month: the Seventh Circuit upheld Illinois’, the en banc Third struck New Jersey’s, and now the First upholds Massachusetts’. What hasn’t happened: no nationwide rule, nothing that changes any other state’s law. The Court hears the Connecticut and Cook County bans (Viramontes/Grant) next term. (Single-outlet reporting, but the opinion is quoted verbatim and the primary CA1 PDF is linked.)

Resist any “the courts are rigged” framing from either direction — the disagreement is the point, and the map is RTB’s value.

New ▲ DOJ · D. Virgin Islands Heat68▲▲AmmoLand (motion quoted; single-outlet)

DOJ moves to block the Virgin Islands’ Act 9113 — arguing the Third Circuit’s Cheeseman already makes a 15-round cap unconstitutional

In a July 22 preliminary-injunction motion filed within its existing suit United States v. Government of the Virgin Islands, the Justice Department asked the court to block two provisions of Act 9113 (signed by Gov. Albert Bryan Jr. on June 24): a ban on magazines holding more than 15 rounds and a bar on selling or transferring firearms “manufactured to hold” more than 15 rounds — which, DOJ argues, reaches standard AR-15s that leave the factory with 20- or 30-round magazines. Because the territory sits within the Third Circuit, DOJ contends the en banc Cheeseman/ANJRPC ruling controls: “ANJRPC squarely holds that this is unconstitutional.” The motion flags that the act grandfathers existing firearms but not magazines — a “Hobson’s choice” to destroy lawful property or risk prosecution — and cites the Tenth Circuit’s Ortega v. Grisham (“the right to bear arms requires a right to acquire arms”) and the pattern-or-practice statute, 34 U.S.C. §12601. DOJ asks the court to protect magazines holding 30 rounds or fewer and to hear the motion at the arguments already set for August 19. No ruling yet. (Single-outlet: AmmoLand, quoting the filing.)

RTB angle: This pairs cleanly with the First Circuit card as the “how a circuit precedent travels” explainer — why a Third Circuit ruling reaches a Caribbean territory, and what “common use” and “the right to acquire” mean in practice. Note it is distinct from GOA’s separate nonresident-carry suit (also USVI). Factual, filing-anchored; no outcome prediction before the Aug 19 hearing.
