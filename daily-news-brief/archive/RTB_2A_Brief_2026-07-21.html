Day Zero in Virginia: as of this morning, no law-enforcement agency in the Commonwealth may enforce the “assault firearms” and magazine bans — and the Fifth Circuit just opened a second front, agreeing to reconsider whether Congress’s commerce power can sustain the federal gun-ban statute at all.

What’s new. The Santolla v. Katz statewide preliminary injunction is operative today: Judge Campbell’s cross-confirmed order bars every Virginia law-enforcement agency and Commonwealth’s Attorney from enforcing the hardware bans, and the held orders dealers have been queueing since early July begin shipping. What changes today is enforcement, not the statute — the law stays on the books while the injunction holds. What doesn’t change: the companion carry ban remains separately delayed, background-check and other transfer rules are untouched, and the Commonwealth’s appeal is still pending — a preliminary injunction with an appeal behind it is a floor that can move. The AG’s July 15 Crump motion (“AR-15s aren’t protected arms”) confirms Virginia intends to keep defending the laws. No day-of reporting had posted at press time; treat operational details as of last confirmed coverage.

The second story: the Fifth Circuit takes Justice Thomas up on his challenge. On July 20 the full Fifth Circuit vacated its panel opinion in United States v. Squire and granted rehearing en banc — transforming, in Judge Higginson’s dissenting words, “what was a Second Amendment as-applied challenge to §922(g)(1)” into “a facial Commerce Clause challenge.” The question: whether a firearm’s having once crossed a state line lets Congress criminalize purely local possession forever — the minimal-nexus theory underlying every §922(g) prosecution. Justice Thomas invited exactly this reexamination in his Hemani concurrence three weeks ago; Higginson: “Already, our court answers the call.” Discipline note: the court struck down nothing — it agreed to ask the question. But a ruling against the government would implicate every prohibited-person category in Texas, Louisiana, and Mississippi and create an instant split. (Single-source: AmmoLand, built on the order and dissent; second source expected.)

The third story: the order list came and went — no 2A action. Monday’s first summer order list produced one new grant — Jouppi v. Alaska, an Eighth Amendment excessive-fines challenge to a $95,000 airplane forfeiture over a six-pack of beer — and no movement on the held magazine petitions (Duncan, Gator’s) or Calce. The quiet outcome resets the Court watch to the Aug 17 list while Viramontes/Grant briefing proceeds toward October argument — now against a live circuit split. (Cross-confirmed: SCOTUSblog + the Court’s posted order list.)

CRS hands Congress its Hemani roadmap: narrow the marijuana-user ban — and “consider modifying other categorical prohibitions” in §922(g)

The Congressional Research Service report, as described in AmmoLand’s July 20 write-up, does two things. What Hemani did not do: erase §922(g)(3), legalize armed intoxication, or resolve the “addicted to” prong. New ▲ 5th Circuit · §922(g) · Commerce Clause Heat83▲▲▲July 20 · AmmoLand (single-source; order + dissent quoted)

The en banc Fifth Circuit vacates U.S. v. Squire and reframes it as a facial Commerce Clause challenge to §922(g)(1) — “already, our court answers the call”

The two-page July 20 order gives no reasons, but Judge Higginson’s dissent spells out the stakes: three weeks after Justice Thomas’s Hemani concurrence asked lower courts to reexamine whether the Commerce Clause supports §922(g), the full Fifth Circuit erased a panel opinion upholding Curtis Squire’s felon-in-possession conviction and took the question en banc. The theory under review is the minimal nexus: a gun that once crossed a state line supports federal jurisdiction over purely local possession forever. The facts are unsympathetic by design — Squire’s record includes heroin-trafficking and burglary convictions, and the gun (unconnected to the shooting that prompted the search) was found in his home — which is precisely why the case tests the principle rather than the defendant. What hasn’t happened: no statute struck, no conviction reversed, no timeline set. A government loss would implicate every §922(g) category in TX/LA/MS, split the circuits instantly, and put the question on a rocket to SCOTUS. The panel opinion’s reliance on colonial laws disarming dissidents and religious minorities — now vacated — is a detail worth remembering when the historical-analogue debate resumes. (Single-source: AmmoLand; the order and briefing schedule are checkable on the CA5 docket.)

RTB angle: This is a doctrine story, not an action item — nothing changes for any member today.
