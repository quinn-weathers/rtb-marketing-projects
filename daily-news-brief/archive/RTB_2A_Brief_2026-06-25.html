DOJ threatens to sue California over the “Glock ban” (AB 1127) and handgun roster — June 30 deadline to negotiate or face a federal complaint

AAG Harmeet Dhillon (DOJ Civil Rights Division) sent Governor Gavin Newsom and AG Rob Bonta a letter Wednesday stating she has authorized a federal complaint against AB 1127 — California’s first-in-the-nation ban on “semiautomatic machinegun-convertible” pistols that use Glock’s cruciform trigger-bar design — and the state’s handgun roster. The letter offers pre-suit settlement talks but warns that if California does not respond by 5:00 p.m. ET on June 30, the United States “may file its complaint without further notice.” DOJ argues both measures bar “the most popular handgun in America,” framing them as a Second Amendment “pattern or practice” violation — the same structure used against DC and Colorado AR-15/magazine bans, the LA County Sheriff, and the US Virgin Islands. A federal court previously found the roster unconstitutional before the Ninth Circuit stayed that ruling. Dhillon’s “at a minimum” ask: agree not to enforce, concede unconstitutionality, and enter a consent decree. Maryland, Connecticut, and New York have since copied California’s Glock-ban design.

Cert watch: could the Comeaux silencer ruling finally push SCOTUS to take Duncan v. Bonta? The term’s final conference is this week

SAF’s Kostas Moros argues the Fifth Circuit’s holding that suppressors are “Arms” (Comeaux) is logically irreconcilable with the Ninth Circuit’s Duncan v. Bonta magazine-ban reasoning, which waved off magazines as benign “accoutrements” not “taken into the hands… to cast at or strike another.” The same logic, Moros notes, applies to suppressors — so the circuits now appear to disagree on a major question, the kind of split that, under Supreme Court Rule 10, makes review more likely. If the plaintiffs file a supplemental brief flagging the new split before the term’s final conference this week, it could improve the odds of a grant. Status: pending — a grant, a denial, or another relist are all possible.

DECIDED last week: Supreme Court unanimously ruled for the marijuana-using gun owner in US v. Hemani — now settling into the record

On June 18 the Court handed down a unanimous decision (Gorsuch writing) holding that the federal government failed to justify prosecuting Ali Hemani under 18 U.S.C. §922(g)(3) — the ban on gun possession by “unlawful users” of controlled substances — based on his admitted, roughly every-other-day marijuana use. The justices emphasized the ruling is narrow, applying to this defendant, and expressly declined to address bans on addicts or the presently intoxicated, §922(g)(1) felony disarmament, or prosecutions backed by individualized proof of dangerousness. Concurrences: Thomas (Commerce Clause); Jackson, joined by Sotomayor (calling Bruen “unworkable”); and Alito, joined by Kagan (urging an even narrower disposition). With ~3/4 of Americans in states with legal marijuana, the practical reach is significant even though the holding is formally narrow.

RTB angle: Keep the “what Hemani actually decided — and what it didn’t” decoder live. Emphasize the narrowness: this is not blanket permission, §922(g)(1) and addiction/intoxication bans are untouched, and individualized-dangerousness prosecutions remain open. Calm, precise, member-facing legal clarity — not a victory lap.
