Top of mind07 · 16

After 45 days of floor silence, Congress finally put points on the gun-policy scoreboard: the House passed H.R. 1181, the Protecting Privacy in Purchases Act, 221–201 — a bill that would bar financial institutions from using gun-retailer-specific merchant category codes nationwide, overriding the CA/CO/NY mandates that took effect in December.

What’s new. Tuesday’s vote drew five Democrats (Cuellar, Davis, Golden, Gray, Gluesenkamp Perez) and Independent Kevin Kiley alongside 215 Republicans; Rep. Brian Fitzpatrick (R-PA) was the lone Republican no. The bill ends — if it becomes law — the eight-year fight that began with a 2018 New York Times column urging credit-card companies to flag “suspicious” gun purchases, ran through the ISO’s 2022 creation of a gun-store MCC, dueling state mandates and bans, and the card networks’ December 2025 rollout of MCC 5723 in the three mandate states. Co-sponsor Rep. Richard Hudson: “Law-abiding gun owners should not have their purchases tracked.” Brady’s Kris Brown counters that “Merchant Category Codes save lives.” The Senate companion, S. 1715 (Hagerty, 24 Republican co-sponsors), sits in Banking with no hearing scheduled — the crossover House vote is the pro-passage camp’s best talking point, but 60 votes remain the wall.

The second story: yesterday’s biggest Unverified is now confirmed. The senate.gov homepage carries an official “Lindsey Graham In Memoriam” section — cross-confirming the death of Sen. Lindsey Graham (R-SC) that SCOTUSblog’s testimony coverage first referenced. That settles the fact; what remains open (and unreported so far) is everything downstream: the South Carolina appointment, and sponsorship of the NSSF-backed FFL Protection Act (S. 4671), which Graham introduced. Content teams should treat the death as confirmed, and everything else as not-yet-reported.

The third story: the NFA registry fight is now a coordinated, multi-court campaign. A day after the four-group Machamer amicus, SAF announced supplemental filings in three civil cases — Brown v. ATF, Jensen v. ATF (part of the consolidated Silencer Shop Foundation v. ATF litigation in Texas) and Roberts v. ATF — arguing that Landor, Wolford and Hemani together strip the $0-tax registry of both its taxing-power rationale and its claimed exemption from Bruen analysis, and that Wolford abrogates the Fifth Circuit’s Peterson licensing analogy. Criminal flank (Machamer) plus civil flank (three districts): the registry question is being teed up for the Supreme Court from multiple directions at once. Meanwhile the Eighth Circuit gave the first big post-Hemani answer in U.S. v. Baxter — unanimously upholding a §922(g)(3) conviction where the record showed individualized dangerousness, exactly the door Gorsuch left open.

New ▲ Federal · House · Passed Heat77▲ July 15 · The Reload (Gutowski)

House passes H.R. 1181, the Protecting Privacy in Purchases Act, 221–201 — a nationwide ban on gun-retailer merchant category codes heads to a Senate where the companion bill has no hearing scheduled

The bill would bar financial institutions from using MCCs unique to firearm retailers, pre-empting the California/Colorado/New York mandates under which the card networks finally implemented MCC 5723 in December 2025 (per Bank of America’s merchant guide, only in states where legally required). Five Democrats — Cuellar, Davis, Golden, Gray, Gluesenkamp Perez — plus Independent Kevin Kiley joined 215 Republicans; Fitzpatrick (R-PA) was the lone Republican no. Hudson: gun owners “should not have their purchases tracked”; Brady’s Kris Brown: the codes “save lives” and can flag traffickers. Notably, MCC data shows only merchant-level spending totals, not itemized purchases — a technical point both sides’ framings tend to blur. S. 1715 (Hagerty + 24 R co-sponsors) sits in Senate Banking. (Single-source: The Reload, with congress.gov bill/vote links; congress.gov unreachable this run.)

SAF opens the civil flank of the NFA registry fight — supplemental filings in Brown, Jensen (Silencer Shop Foundation consol.) and Roberts v. ATF argue Landor, Wolford and Hemani leave the $0-tax registry without constitutional authority

The filings make a two-part attack. Taxing power: with the One Big Beautiful Bill zeroing the suppressor/SBR/SBS/AOW making-and-transfer tax, maintaining registration on untaxed citizens would let Congress “regulate in minute detail the activities of untaxed Americans” — and Landor, SAF argues, forecloses stretching Necessary-and-Proper into a police power. Second Amendment: suppressed firearms and SBRs are “arms,” so Wolford/Hemani put the historical-tradition burden on the government — and Wolford “leaves no room for a carveout,” abrogating (they contend) the Fifth Circuit’s Peterson shall-issue-licensing analogy, since “registration… focuses on tracking firearms and who owns them.” Sack: the rulings “had a direct impact on SAF’s ongoing NFA challenges.” Combined with the Machamer criminal-appeal amicus, the registry question now advances on four boards at once. (Single-source: AmmoLand, built on SAF’s press release; filing texts not yet independently reviewed.)

New ▲ 8th Circuit · §922(g)(3) Heat57▲ July 14 · The Reload (Fogleman) + opinion PDF

First major post-Hemani test: the Eighth Circuit unanimously upholds a marijuana-user gun conviction — individualized dangerousness, the door Gorsuch left open, sustains §922(g)(3) in U.S. v. Baxter

Three weeks after the Supreme Court unanimously tossed similar charges in Hemani (regular user, no showing of danger), Judge Shepherd’s panel affirmed Keshon Baxter’s conviction and 64-month sentence: the government “met its burden” of showing Baxter’s conduct analogous to Founding-era “Terror of the People” laws — citing expert testimony linking chronic use to aggression, gang altercations while armed, and flight from police. The panel quoted Hemani’s own reservation: the Court did not address prosecutions “accompanied by individualized proof” of danger. The early lesson of the fallout cases: Hemani protects the non-dangerous user, not the category. (Reported by The Reload with the opinion PDF linked — primary source available.)

The merchant-code whiplash, retailer edition — H.R. 1181 would unwind MCC 5723 seven months after the card networks finally implemented it in CA/CO/NY

For FFLs the story is operational, not just political: the industry-specific code took effect in the three mandate states in December 2025 (Bank of America’s merchant guide instructed use “only in those states where it’s legally required”), after years in which Visa and peers paused implementation over the state-law conflict.
