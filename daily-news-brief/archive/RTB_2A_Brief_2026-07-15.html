Top of mind07 · 15

While the AR-15 fight waits for October, the litigation map quietly widened on three new boards. The headline move: the NRA, FPC, SAF and the American Suppressor Association jointly asked the Sixth Circuit to declare the NFA’s SBR registration scheme unconstitutional — arguing 1.18 million registered short-barreled rifles are protected arms and the barrel-length line was a “historical accident.”

What’s new. Four major gun-rights groups filed a July 13 amicus brief in United States v. Machamer (6th Cir.), authored by NRA attorney Joseph G.S. Greenlee. The brief addresses only the NFA’s approval, registration and possession provisions (26 U.S.C. §§5812, 5841, 5861(d)) as applied to SBRs — not Machamer’s separate manufacturing charges. Core arguments: SBRs are “bearable arms” at Heller/Bruen/Wolford step one; the “dangerous and unusual” exception is conjunctive, and 1,178,348 registered SBRs (as of June 8, 2026), lawful in 45 states, are anything but unusual; and the 1934 barrel-length cutoff entered the statute as a safe-harbor drafting artifact, not an anti-crime measure. The government’s analogues (a 1631 Virginia arms census, foreign-arms-sales bans, proof-testing laws) get dismantled in turn. FPC’s Brandon Combs aimed the political edge at the administration: “The Trump DOJ wants courts to treat the Second Amendment like a second-class right.”

Why it matters. This is the criminal-law flank of the post-“$0 tax” NFA fight: with the transfer tax zeroed out and NFA checks up +177% YoY, the registry’s revenue rationale is gone, and Machamer squarely presents whether a paperwork regime without historical support can still send someone to federal prison. AmmoLand frames it as one of three NFA lawsuits putting the SBR/suppressor registry on a path to the Supreme Court — a doctrinal track running parallel to (and largely independent of) Viramontes. Alignment note for content: the NRA and FPC are simultaneously fighting alongside DOJ in hardware-ban cases and against DOJ here — a nuance worth explaining precisely.

The second story: “defend by distinction” becomes the states’ pattern. Maryland’s July 13 answer in Bathras v. Moore gives the first look at its SB 334 “Glock ban” defense: certain Glocks are “semiautomatic firearms that are capable of being fully automatic weapons” — treating a lawful factory pistol as its hypothetical illegally-converted twin — while pleading “without knowledge” on Glock’s common-use evidence and offering no historical tradition yet. Same day, Delaware asked the federal court to keep Birney (the HB 451 under-21 ban challenge) frozen, arguing the Third Circuit’s binding Lara ruling covers only carry, not purchase/possession — a distinction SAF’s Kostas Moros calls “unserious” and the Fifth Circuit’s Reese and Tenth Circuit’s Ortega (“one cannot keep or bear arms if one cannot acquire them”) cut against.

New ▲ 6th Circuit · NFA · SBRs Heat72▲ July 14 · AmmoLand (single-source)

NRA, FPC, SAF and the American Suppressor Association tell the Sixth Circuit the NFA’s SBR registry is unconstitutional — 1.18 million registered SBRs, lawful in 45 states, and a barrel-length line born as a “historical accident”

The four groups’ July 13 amicus in U.S. v. Machamer (NRA’s Joseph G.S. Greenlee authoring) challenges the NFA’s approval/registration/possession provisions as applied to SBRs. The district court had held SBRs “unusual or dangerous” and thus not “Arms” — the brief says that gets both steps wrong: Heller/Bruen/Wolford make the plain-text step about bearable arms, and the exception is “dangerous and unusual,” conjunctive. With 1,178,348 registered SBRs (June 8 count), commonality is hard to dispute. On history: the barrel-length minimum entered the 1934 Act as a safe harbor to protect hunting rifles from the concealable-firearm tax language — “no one mentioned a short-barreled rifle having any criminal use” in the Senate hearings — and the government’s analogues (a 1631 Virginia census, foreign-sales bans, proof-testing laws) involve no advance-permission registry. Miller (1939, uncontested, shotgun) doesn’t freeze the analysis. Combs (FPC): “The Trump DOJ wants courts to treat the Second Amendment like a second-class right.” (Single-source: AmmoLand, editorial-inflected; filing facts treated as fact, characterizations as commentary.)

New ▲ Maryland · Glock ban · SB 334 Heat64▲ July 13 · AmmoLand (single-source)

Maryland files its first defense of the SB 334 “Glock ban” in Bathras v. Moore — Glock-style pistols can be singled out as “capable of being fully automatic,” while the state pleads “without knowledge” on common-use evidence

Maryland’s July 13 answer frames its theory: cruciform-trigger-bar pistols (nearly every Glock, plus the PSA Dagger, Ruger RXM, Shadow Systems and similar) are different from other semiautomatic handguns because illegal converters can make them fire automatically. The NRA/FPC/SAF plaintiffs — joined by a June 25 amended complaint adding two Maryland gun owners and Atlantic Guns (Rockville, selling since 1950) — counter that the converter is already contraband under both federal and Maryland law, the unmodified pistol fires once per trigger pull, and “that is a handgun ban” under Heller. Notably, the state responds to Glock market-share, production and law-enforcement-issue allegations (including Baltimore PD) by pleading “without knowledge or information,” and its answer identifies no historical tradition yet. The ban takes effect January 1, 2027. Pairs with California’s AB 1127 fight as a two-coast test of the “convertible” theory. (Single-source: AmmoLand.)
