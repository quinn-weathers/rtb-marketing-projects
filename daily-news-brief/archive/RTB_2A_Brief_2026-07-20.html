Top of mind07 · 20

The dam breaks: an en banc Third Circuit struck down New Jersey’s AR-15 and magazine bans 10–5 on Friday — the first federal appeals court ever to invalidate a state “assault weapon” or magazine ban, creating a direct circuit split just weeks before the Supreme Court takes up the same question in Viramontes.

What’s new. In a 192-page decision in ANJRPC v. Attorney General of New Jersey, Judge Arianna Freeman — a Biden appointee, writing for a cross-party 10-judge majority — held that semi-automatic rifles like the AR-15 and magazines over ten rounds are “plainly in common use” for lawful purposes, and that no historical tradition supports banning them: “Bans on weapons in common use for lawful purposes are unlawful. So are severe restrictions.” The ruling covers all semi-automatic rifles (the record was too thin on covered pistols and shotguns), squarely rejects the “AR-15s aren’t arms” theory, and partially reverses Judge Sheridan’s narrower 2024 decision. Judge Shwartz’s four-judge dissent argues AR-15s and LCMs fall outside the Second Amendment’s scope. FPC calls it “a massive victory”; NJ AG Jennifer Davenport calls it “as unfortunate as it is legally incorrect” and says the state is “considering our options” — which now run through a Supreme Court that granted Viramontes/Grant on June 30. Cross-confirmed: The Reload, AmmoLand, opinion PDF (primary source).

The second story: Virginia’s injunction takes effect tomorrow — and the Commonwealth just showed its hand. T-1 to the July 21 statewide Santolla injunction, AG Jay Jones’s office filed a motion to dismiss in Crump v. Katz arguing Virginia’s constitution protects only a militia-tethered collective right, that AR-15s and standard magazines are not protected “arms,” and that any burden is outweighed by the state’s interest — interest-balancing language Bruen rejected, filed 15 days after SCOTUS granted cert on exactly this question and two days before the Third Circuit answered it the other way. The collision of arguments is itself the story.

The third story: DOJ folds on the post-office carry ban. On July 16 the Justice Department moved (unopposed) to dismiss its Fifth Circuit appeal in FPC v. Blanche, leaving Judge O’Connor’s permanent injunction intact: SAF and FPC members may carry at ordinary post offices. No reason given in the 41-word motion; the sequence — DOJ asked to hold the appeal for Wolford, lost that request, Wolford came down June 25, DOJ quit three weeks later — suggests the “sensitive place” theory didn’t survive. Note the limits: the injunction protects SAF/FPC members at ordinary post offices, not everyone everywhere, and no circuit precedent is created. (Single-source: AmmoLand, built on the court filing.)

Virginia’s motion to dismiss in Crump v. Katz: a collective-right theory, “AR-15s aren’t arms,” and interest-balancing — filed 15 days after SCOTUS granted cert on the same question

AG Jay Jones’s July 15 filing argues Article I, §13 of Virginia’s constitution protects only a militia-tethered right; that even under an individual-right reading, “assault firearms” and 15+ round magazines are not protected “arms” because they are “most useful in military service” and “not in common use today for lawful self-defense”; and that the state’s “substantial” public-safety interest outweighs a “modest” burden — language tracking the Fourth Circuit’s Kolbe intermediate-scrutiny test that Bruen repudiated.
