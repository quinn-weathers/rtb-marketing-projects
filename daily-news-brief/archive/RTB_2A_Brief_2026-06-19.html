SCOTUS Monday orders: cert denied in Corbett v. Hochul and NSSF v. James; four 2A cases granted, 25 relisted

In Monday’s orders the Court declined to take up two New York challenges. Corbett v. Watch ▼ Courts · Washington Heat60▼ June 19 · The Reload (Fogleman), WA Supreme Court

Washington Supreme Court (5–4): repeat DUI convictions can trigger disarmament without an individualized dangerousness finding

In McLellan & Holloway v. Brown, the Washington Supreme Court held that the state does not violate the Second Amendment by temporarily disarming people convicted of two or more DUIs within seven years under a 2023 law (the bar is indefinite, with a petition for restoration available after five law-abiding years). Writing for the majority, Justice Steven González concluded the legislature may disarm a category of people based on conviction type — without proving each individual is dangerous — pointing to historical traditions of disarming “dangerous” groups and distinguishing Rahimi. The four-justice dissent (Justice Helen Whitner) countered that there is “no historical analog for driving under the influence” and that the law disarms people “before they commit a violent crime on the assumption that one day they might.” The holding is narrow on its facts, but the categorical-dangerousness reasoning could carry far — and a similar federal question is awaiting en banc review at the Third Circuit (Williams).

RTB angle: A careful, evenhanded explainer: “what the Washington DUI ruling actually holds — and what it doesn’t.” RTB’s value is precision: it’s narrow, it’s split 5–4, and it sits against a possible SCOTUS signal in Hemani. No alarm; just clarity.
