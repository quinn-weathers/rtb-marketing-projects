Top of mind07 · 09

Virginia’s gun ban is now blocked statewide. A Washington County judge expanded the Santolla v. Katz injunction into a universal injunction covering every law-enforcement agency and Commonwealth’s Attorney in Virginia — effective July 21 and set to run through July 2027, past the expected SCOTUS AR-15 decision. The “muddled” patchwork era is scheduled to end in twelve days.

What the court did. Judge Jeffrey L. Campbell ruled that the existing injunction in the NRA-backed Santolla v. Katz should apply universally — barring any law-enforcement agency or Commonwealth’s Attorney from enforcing Virginia’s “assault firearms” and magazine bans while the case proceeds. AG Jay Jones had asked the court to keep the injunction limited to the named defendants; Campbell rejected that, writing that a person could “freely transport an AR-15 through Washington County” yet “arguably…be charged for the same conduct” one county over — a “treacherous patchwork of enforcement” for plaintiffs and police alike. The July 21 effective date gives agencies time to be notified; the order is set to remain in effect through July 2027. It is the second injunction against the law, after Lancaster County’s named-defendant-only order — and the second setback for the Commonwealth this week, after the consolidation denial left it defending the law in multiple courts at once.

Why it matters — and the caveat. Since July 1, RTB has tracked the enforcement picture as a “muddled mess”: injunctions that named only some defendants, FFLs holding inventory out of caution, county-by-county uncertainty. If this order takes effect as reported, that ends July 21 — uniformly, statewide, for the life of the litigation. It is still a preliminary posture, not a merits ruling: the ban remains on the books, and the underlying suits (and DOJ’s parallel federal case) continue. Sourcing caveat: this run the story rests on AmmoLand’s July 8 report (John Crump) plus NRA-ILA Executive Director John Commerford’s quoted statement; independent cross-confirmation (The Reload, court order text) is still pending — treat operational details (especially the July 21 date) as reported, not settled.

The second story: the Third Circuit is moving, not waiting. By a July 6 order, the en banc Third Circuit asked both sides in Cheeseman/ANJRPC — the New Jersey AR-15 and magazine-ban case — for 1,500-word supplemental briefs on the impact of Wolford and Hemani, due July 10 at noon, extensions all but ruled out. The contrast is stark: the Ninth Circuit just vacated submission in Miller v. Bonta to wait for Viramontes, while the Third Circuit appears to be actively working its case — and The Reload’s new analysis on the “reshaped” Third Circuit lands on the same theme. A pre-Viramontes circuit ruling either way would reshape the fall landscape.

Verification note. A materially better sourcing day: AmmoLand and SCOTUSblog were both reachable this run alongside The Reload (whose newest post remains the July 7 Rose report). Still Unverified: the ruling on DOJ’s California AB 1127 TRO; Colorado’s SB25-003 final list and Initiative 89 signature outcome; and independent confirmation of the Virginia statewide-injunction details above.
