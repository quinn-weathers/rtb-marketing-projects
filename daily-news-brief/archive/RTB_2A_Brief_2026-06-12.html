The courts and the Justice Department are now driving the story. The Supreme Court cleared its underbrush Monday — denying review in US v. Cockerham and relisting the Duncan magazine and Viramontes AR-15 cases for a 19th time — while confirming it will hand down Hemani and the marquee carry case Wolford v. Lopez by the end of June. DOJ opened a fresh Second Amendment investigation into the Philadelphia Police, and Virginia’s AG unveiled a striking “collective-right” defense of the hardware ban as the court postponed the hearing — with the ban now T-19 to July 1.

SCOTUS, decoded. Monday’s orders list — the item flagged “unverified” earlier this week — is now confirmed via The Reload. The Court declined to hear US v. Cockerham, a DOJ appeal of a Fifth Circuit ruling (Judge James Ho) that the federal felon-in-possession ban couldn’t permanently disarm a man whose only predicate was failing to pay child support. No justice wrote separately. Notably, the Court did not hold the case pending its decision in US v. Hemani (the marijuana-user gun-ban case) — a break from its recent GVR habit that some read as a possible signal of a ruling favorable to certain non-violent prohibited persons. The Court also relisted Duncan v. Bonta (California magazine ban) and Viramontes v. Cook County (AR-15 ban) for the nineteenth time. Bottom line: Hemani and Wolford v. Lopez are both expected by the end of June — Wolford was not decided this week, so keep the explainer pre-positioned.

DOJ’s 2A campaign reaches Philadelphia. The DOJ Civil Rights Division (AAG Harmeet Dhillon) sent Mayor Cherelle Parker a letter Tuesday opening an investigation into whether the Philadelphia Police revoke carry permits on subjective “good cause” grounds that conflict with Pennsylvania law and Bruen. It’s the latest in an escalating pattern — after Los Angeles, Denver, and Washington, DC — of the administration using federal civil-rights authority to challenge local permitting practices. DOJ says it will sue if it finds a pattern of violations.

Virginia’s novel defense — and a telling delay. In Crump v. Katz, AG Jay Jones argued the Virginia Constitution’s arms provision (§13) protects only a collective, militia-tethered right — not an individual one — and that, even if it did, AR-15s are “dangerous and unusual” and not in common use. He layered on standing and procedural challenges (arguing GOA/VCDL can’t sue for members and that plaintiff John Crump named the wrong official). The three-judge panel then postponed the initial hearing while it weighs consolidating the challenges — an in-no-hurry posture that is a poor sign for gun-rights plaintiffs with the ban T-19 to July 1.

The data story still has runway. Pew’s John Gramlich (on The Reload’s podcast) walked through FBI/CDC figures showing the murder rate falling toward record lows while gun suicides hold a near-record share of gun deaths. It remains under-discussed on mainstream social — still the cleanest, evidence-led content opening on the board.

What this means for RTB posture today. Lead with the courts. Ship a calm, factual Crump v. Katz explainer (what the “collective right” argument is, why the hearing delay matters, what changes July 1) and a short SCOTUS-decoder on Cockerham/Hemani/Wolford. Keep the Wolford explainer ready for an end-of-June drop, and treat the Philadelphia probe as national context for the “your permit, your rights” theme. Calm and precise beats loud.

Moved ▲ State · Virginia Heat85▲ June 12 · The Reload (Gutowski), AmmoLand, court filing

Virginia hardware ban (SB749) — AG’s novel “collective-right” defense in Crump v. Katz; hearing postponed; T-19 to July 1

Attorney General Jay Jones filed his opposition to a preliminary injunction in Crump v. Katz with a striking lead argument: that Virginia’s constitutional arms provision (§13, adopted 1971) protects only a collective, militia-tethered right — not an individual one — pointing to the word “therefore,” the section’s placement among military matters, and its ratification record. In the alternative, he argued AR-15s and 15+ round magazines are “dangerous and unusual,” not “in common use” (disputing the tens-of-millions estimates), and that even grenade-launcher and suppressor-capable configurations defeat the facial challenge. He also raised procedural defenses: that GOA and VCDL lack authority to sue for their members, and that plaintiff (AmmoLand writer John Crump) sued the wrong official (State Police Superintendent Katz rather than local prosecutors). Notably, the three-judge panel postponed the initial hearing while it weighs consolidating the four challenges — an unhurried posture with the ban now T-19 to July 1. Demand backdrop unchanged: May sales roughly doubled (#2 long-gun), with at least 14 prosecutors declining to enforce.
