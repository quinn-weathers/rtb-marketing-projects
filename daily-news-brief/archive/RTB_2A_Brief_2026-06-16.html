Watch ▼ Courts · Washington Heat75▼ June 16 · The Reload (Fogleman), WA Supreme Court

Washington Supreme Court (5–4): repeat DUI convictions can trigger disarmament without an individualized dangerousness finding

In McLellan & Holloway v. Brown, the Washington Supreme Court held that the state does not violate the Second Amendment by temporarily disarming people convicted of two or more DUIs within seven years under a 2023 law (the bar is indefinite, with a petition for restoration available after five law-abiding years). Writing for the majority, Justice Steven González concluded the legislature may disarm a category of people based on conviction type — without proving each individual is dangerous — pointing to historical traditions of disarming “dangerous” groups and barring possession by the actively intoxicated, and distinguishing the U.S. Supreme Court’s Rahimi. The four-justice dissent (Justice Helen Whitner) countered that there is “no historical analog for driving under the influence” and that the law disarms people “before they commit a violent crime on the assumption that one day they might.” The holding is narrow on its facts, but the categorical-dangerousness reasoning could carry far — and a similar federal question over DUI-based disarmament is awaiting en banc review at the Third Circuit (Williams).

RTB angle: A careful, evenhanded explainer: “what the Washington DUI ruling actually holds — and what it doesn’t.” This is a state-court reading of a fast-moving constitutional question (who can be disarmed, and on what proof) that members will see headlines about. RTB’s value is precision: it’s narrow, it’s split 5–4, and it sits against a possible SCOTUS signal in Hemani. No alarm; just clarity.
